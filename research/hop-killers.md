## Overview

### The Concept

The "HOp Killers" concept was developed by Dr. Scott Weingart (EMCrit) as part of the LAMW (Laryngoscope as a Murder Weapon) series. The original triad was:
- **H**ypotension
- **O**xygenation (Hypoxemia)
- **p**H (Severe Metabolic Acidosis)

The concept has since expanded to include:
- Right Heart Failure (especially massive PE)
- Severe Metabolic Derangements (hyperkalemia, etc.)
- Elevated ICP

### Why Standard RSI Kills These Patients

Standard RSI involves simultaneous administration of a sedative and paralytic with no ventilations until intubation. This sequence is dangerous in physiologically unstable patients because:

1. **Sedatives drop SVR** - Loss of sympathetic tone causes vasodilation and hypotension
2. **Positive pressure ventilation drops preload** - PPV reduces venous return to the heart
3. **Apnea loses respiratory compensation** - In acidotic patients, CO2 rises rapidly during apnea
4. **Paralysis prevents spontaneous breathing** - Patients cannot self-rescue from hypoxemia

### Key Statistics

- Nearly 40% of ED/ICU intubations are complicated by hypoxemia, hypotension, or cardiac arrest
- Cardiac arrest is more common in patients with pre-intubation hypotension (12% vs 3%; p < 0.002)
- IVC collapsibility index of 40-45% predicts post-induction hypotension

### Screening Questions to Identify Killers

Before any intubation, ask:
1. What is the blood pressure? (SBP < 100 = concern)
2. What is the oxygen saturation? (SpO2 < 93% = concern)
3. What is the pH? (pH < 7.2 = concern)
4. Any signs of RV failure/PE? (JVD, hypotension, hypoxemia = concern)
5. What is the potassium? (K+ > 5.5 = avoid succinylcholine)
6. Any concern for elevated ICP? (TBI, mass lesion, herniation signs)

## Killer #1: HYPOTENSION

### Why RSI Kills

- **Sedatives drop SVR**: Propofol, etomidate, and even ketamine can drop blood pressure in catecholamine-depleted patients
- **PPV drops preload**: Positive pressure ventilation reduces venous return
- **Loss of sympathetic drive**: Critically ill patients rely on endogenous catecholamines to maintain perfusion

### Which Patients

- Septic shock (vasodilated, preload-dependent)
- Hemorrhagic shock (hypovolemic, preload-dependent)
- Cardiogenic shock (pump failure, afterload-sensitive)
- Neurogenic shock (loss of sympathetic tone)
- Any patient on vasopressors pre-intubation

### Pre-Intubation Resuscitation Targets

| Target | Goal | Why |
|--------|------|-----|
| **MAP** | > 65 mmHg (ideally > 75) | Ensure adequate perfusion before hemodynamic insult |
| **SBP** | > 140 mmHg | Provides buffer for post-induction drop |
| **Volume** | 1-2L crystalloid if hypovolemic | Optimize preload BEFORE intubation |
| **Access** | 2 large-bore proximal IVs | Rapid fluid/pressor administration |

### ROCKETamine Protocol (Shock Dose RSI)

**Principle:** Dose sedatives LOW and paralytics HIGH

| Medication | Standard Dose | Shock Dose | Notes |
|------------|---------------|------------|-------|
| **Ketamine** | 1-2 mg/kg | 0.5 mg/kg | Additional 0.5 mg/kg PRN until sedated |
| **Rocuronium** | 1.0-1.2 mg/kg | 1.6 mg/kg | Higher dose ensures rapid onset, longer safe apnea |

**Why Ketamine?**
- Least cardio-depressant induction agent
- Maintains SVR via sympathetic stimulation
- However, in catecholamine-depleted patients, even ketamine can cause hypotension
- Reduce dose and be prepared with push-dose pressors

### Push-Dose Pressors

**Epinephrine (preferred):**
- Provides BOTH alpha (vasoconstriction) AND beta (inotropic) effects
- Preparation: 1 mL of 1:10,000 (100 mcg) + 9 mL NS = 10 mcg/mL
- Dose: 5-20 mcg (0.5-2 mL) IV push every 2-5 minutes PRN
- Give 5 mcg with induction meds prophylactically in high-risk patients

**Phenylephrine:**
- Pure alpha agonist (vasoconstriction only)
- Preparation: 1 mL of phenylephrine (10 mg/mL) + 99 mL NS = 100 mcg/mL
- Dose: 50-200 mcg (0.5-2 mL) IV push every 2-5 minutes PRN
- Use when tachycardia is a concern
- Note: May slow sedative onset compared to epinephrine

### Post-Intubation Hypotension Management

1. **Start norepinephrine infusion immediately** - Can run peripherally via proximal IV for up to 4 hours
2. **Start at 5-10 mcg/min**, titrate to MAP > 65
3. **Continue push-dose pressors** as bridge while infusion takes effect
4. **Expedite central line** but do not delay vasopressors
5. **Consider volume** if truly hypovolemic, but avoid fluid overload

### Awake Intubation Alternative

- Preserves endogenous catecholamines by maintaining spontaneous ventilation
- Topicalize with lidocaine:
  - 10 mL of 4% lidocaine sprayed oropharyngeally
  - 2-4% applied to posterior tongue
  - 5 mL sprayed past vocal cords
- Consider for profound shock where any sedation may be fatal

## Killer #2: HYPOXIA

### Why RSI Kills

- **Cannot tolerate apnea**: Patients with shunt physiology (ARDS, pneumonia) desaturate within seconds of apnea
- **Cannot preoxygenate adequately**: Agitated hypoxic patients won't tolerate masks
- **Desaturation during laryngoscopy**: Even brief attempts can cause critical hypoxemia

### Which Patients

- ARDS
- Severe pneumonia
- Pulmonary edema
- Morbid obesity
- Pulmonary embolism
- COVID-19 pneumonia
- Any patient with SpO2 < 93% despite supplemental O2

### NODESAT Protocol

**N** - Nasal O2 (15 lpm or HFNC 60 lpm)
**O** - Optimal positioning (head elevated, ramped)
**D** - Delayed Sequence Intubation (if patient won't cooperate)
**E** - End-tidal CO2 monitoring
**S** - Suction ready
**A** - Apneic oxygenation during laryngoscopy
**T** - Two-person BVM technique

### Preoxygenation Technique: Rule of 15s

All patients receive:
1. **Nasal cannula at 15 lpm** (leave on during laryngoscopy for apneic oxygenation)
2. **Non-rebreather mask at 15+ lpm** OR
3. **CPAP/BiPAP at up to 15 cmH2O** (if SpO2 cannot reach 100%)

For patients requiring PEEP:
- BVM + PEEP valve (5-15 cmH2O) + NC at 15 lpm
- Tight seal, jaw thrust, NO active bagging (Apneic CPAP Recruitment)

### Delayed Sequence Intubation (DSI)

**Concept:** Procedural sedation for preoxygenation. The procedure IS effective preoxygenation.

**Indications:**
- Agitated/delirious hypoxic patient unable to tolerate mask-based preoxygenation
- SpO2 < 93% despite maximal preoxygenation attempts
- Patient fighting NIV/mask

**Medication Options:**

| Agent | Dose | Onset | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|------|-------|------------|---------------|
| **Ketamine** | 1-2 mg/kg IV | 30 seconds | Preserves airway reflexes, spontaneous ventilation | Sympathomimetic (may worsen HTN/tachycardia) |
| **Dexmedetomidine** | 1 mcg/kg over 10 min | 3-5 minutes | No respiratory depression, no sympathomimetic effects | Slower onset, may cause bradycardia |

**DSI Protocol:**
1. Push ketamine 1-2 mg/kg IV (or dex infusion)
2. Wait 30-60 seconds for dissociation
3. Apply preoxygenation (NRB, CPAP, or NIV)
4. Continue for 2-3 minutes until SpO2 > 95%
5. Check quantitative ETCO2 and document baseline
6. THEN push paralytic
7. Proceed to intubation after 45-60 seconds

**Key Advantage:** Frequently, after sedation and NIV, respiratory parameters improve so dramatically that intubation can be AVOIDED.

### Apneic Oxygenation During Laryngoscopy

| Method | Flow Rate | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Standard NC | 15 lpm | Effective, widely available |
| HFNC | 60 lpm | Longer safe apnea time (11 min vs 7 min), no superiority for preventing desaturation |

**Keep NC in place during laryngoscopy** - This is the key intervention

### HFNC Settings

- Flow: 60 lpm
- FiO2: 100%
- Provides moderate PEEP, dead space washout, high FiO2

### Evidence for DSI

- RCT: Peri-intubation hypoxemia 8% (DSI) vs 35% (standard RSI), p < 0.001
- First-pass success higher with DSI: 83% vs 69%, p = 0.02
- Adjusted OR for hypoxemia with standard RSI: 6.8 (95% CI 2.8-16.5)

### Awake Intubation Indications

- Predicted impossible BVM ventilation
- Predicted impossible surgical airway
- Patients who cannot tolerate ANY apnea
- Consider for severe hypoxemia that cannot be improved with DSI

## Killer #3: SEVERE ACIDOSIS

### Why RSI Kills

- **Respiratory compensation lost during apnea**: Acidotic patients hyperventilate to blow off CO2. During apnea, CO2 rises rapidly (3-6 mmHg/min), pH crashes further.
- **Even brief apnea is dangerous**: A few seconds can worsen pH enough to cause cardiac dysrhythmias
- **Post-intubation ventilator mismanagement**: Standard ventilator settings (RR 12, TV 500) do not maintain the minute ventilation needed

### Which Patients

- DKA (especially pH < 7.1)
- Sepsis with severe lactic acidosis
- Toxic ingestions (salicylates, methanol, ethylene glycol)
- Renal failure with metabolic acidosis
- Metformin-associated lactic acidosis

### pH Thresholds

| pH | Risk Level | Considerations |
|----|------------|----------------|
| 7.2-7.3 | Moderate | Intubation relatively safe with precautions |
| 7.1-7.2 | High | Consider avoiding intubation if possible |
| < 7.1 | Critical | Intubation may be fatal; maximize pre-intubation optimization |

### Try to Avoid Intubation

- Even a short trial of NIV while treating underlying cause
- NIPPV can maintain respiratory compensation
- BiPAP allows augmented minute ventilation
- Many DKA patients improve dramatically within 1-2 hours with insulin/fluids

### Winter's Equation

Calculate the expected compensatory PaCO2:

**Expected PaCO2 = (1.5 x HCO3) + 8 (+/- 2)**

Example: pH 6.9, HCO3 5 mEq/L
- Expected PaCO2 = (1.5 x 5) + 8 = 15.5 mmHg (+/- 2)
- Patient must maintain PaCO2 of 13-18 to compensate
- Standard ventilator settings (PaCO2 ~40) would be catastrophic

### VAPOX Technique (Ventilator-Assisted Pre-Oxygenation)

**Equipment:**
- ICU ventilator brought to bedside (NOT a BiPAP machine)
- Non-invasive ventilation mask
- Someone who knows how to adjust the ventilator in real time

**Pre-Intubation Settings (Pseudo-NIV Phase):**

| Parameter | Setting | Rationale |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Mode | SIMV | Allows spontaneous breaths |
| Tidal Volume | 550 mL (8 mL/kg IBW) | Standard |
| FiO2 | 100% | Maximize oxygenation |
| Flow Rate | 30 lpm | Slow to prevent gastric insufflation |
| PSV | 5-15 cmH2O | Augments spontaneous breaths |
| PEEP | 5 cmH2O (increase to 15 if SpO2 > 95%) | Maintain oxygenation |
| RR | 0 | Patient breathing spontaneously |

**Attach quantitative ETCO2 monitoring and document baseline value.**

### During RSI (Apneic Phase)

After pushing induction + paralytic:
1. Increase RR to 12 on ventilator
2. Perform jaw thrust
3. Wait 45 seconds (medications onset)
4. **The ventilator is breathing for the patient through the NIV mask** during this apneic window

### Post-Intubation Ventilator Settings

**Immediately after tube placement:**

| Parameter | Setting | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| RR | 30 breaths/min | Match pre-intubation hyperventilation |
| TV | 8 mL/kg IBW | Standard |
| Flow Rate | 60 lpm | Standard intubated setting |
| FiO2 | 100% initially | Wean as tolerated |

**Target:** ETCO2 at least as low as it was before intubation

**Get ABG urgently post-intubation** - Do not wait!

### Sodium Bicarbonate

**Controversial and generally NOT recommended:**
- Bicarbonate generates CO2 when it buffers acid
- Acidotic patients are already maximally hyperventilating
- Additional CO2 cannot be blown off, may worsen intracellular acidosis
- Recent ICU study (BICAR-ICU): No benefit in severe metabolic acidemia except possibly in AKI

**If given:** Only in patients with adequate ventilatory reserve who are NOT being intubated

## Killer #4: RIGHT HEART FAILURE

### Why RSI Kills

- **PPV increases RV afterload**: Positive intrathoracic pressure is transmitted to pulmonary vasculature
- **PPV drops RV preload**: Reduced venous return to already struggling RV
- **Sedatives drop SVR**: RV perfusion depends on systemic pressure
- **Result: RV death spiral**

### The RV Death Spiral

1. Increased RV afterload (PE, PPV, hypoxia, hypercarbia)
2. RV dilation
3. Septal shift toward LV
4. Decreased LV filling
5. Decreased cardiac output
6. Decreased coronary perfusion
7. RV ischemia
8. Further RV dysfunction
9. Death

### Which Patients

- Massive pulmonary embolism
- Known pulmonary hypertension
- Cor pulmonale
- Severe COPD with RV strain
- Acute ARDS with RV failure
- RV infarction

### Pre-Intubation Stabilization

**AVOID INTUBATION IF AT ALL POSSIBLE**

- NIV, basic airway maneuvers first
- Intubation is truly a last resort in these patients

**If intubation unavoidable:**

| Intervention | Details | Rationale |
|--------------|---------|-----------|
| **Vasopressors running** | Norepinephrine or epinephrine infusion | Maintain systemic pressure > pulmonary pressure |
| **Push-dose pressors ready** | Epinephrine 10 mcg/mL syringes | Immediate hemodynamic support |
| **Avoid fluid boluses** | Fluid may worsen RV distension | Most RV failure patients are volume OVERLOADED |
| **Consider diuresis** | Paradoxically, removing fluid may improve output | Reduces septal bowing |
| **Correct hypoxia first** | Supplemental O2 is a pulmonary vasodilator | Reduces PVR |
| **Correct acidosis** | Acidosis increases PVR | BiPAP if needed |

### Induction Strategy

**Awake intubation is probably preferred** - Maintains spontaneous ventilation and endogenous catecholamines

**If RSI required:**
- Ketamine 0.5 mg/kg (maintains SVR)
- Have vasopressors infusing BEFORE induction
- Push-dose epinephrine with induction medications

### Avoid These

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| **Hypoxia** | Pulmonary vasoconstriction |
| **Hypercarbia** | Pulmonary vasoconstriction |
| **Acidosis** | Pulmonary vasoconstriction |
| **Propofol** | Drops SVR dramatically |
| **Excessive fluid** | Worsens RV distension |
| **High PEEP** | Increases RV afterload |

### Pulmonary Vasodilators

**Consider starting immediately post-intubation:**

| Agent | Route | Dose | Notes |
|-------|-------|------|-------|
| **Inhaled nitric oxide** | Via ventilator | 20-40 ppm | Expensive but effective |
| **Inhaled epoprostenol** | Via ventilator | 50 ng/kg/min | Alternative to NO |
| **IV nitroglycerin** | Infusion | 10-200 mcg/min | Reduces preload and PVR |

### Post-Intubation Ventilator Strategy

| Parameter | Target | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| **PEEP** | Low (5-8 cmH2O) | Minimize RV afterload increase |
| **TV** | 6-8 mL/kg | Avoid over-distension |
| **Pplat** | < 30 cmH2O | Minimize transpulmonary pressure |
| **FiO2** | 100% initially | Oxygen is original pulmonary vasodilator |
| **RR** | Adequate to avoid hypercarbia | CO2 is pulmonary vasoconstrictor |

### Massive PE-Specific Considerations

| Consideration | Action |
|---------------|--------|
| **Thrombolytics** | Consider before intubation if massive PE with shock |
| **ECMO** | Have conversation early; may need emergent cannulation |
| **Catheter-directed therapy** | If available, may be preferable to systemic lysis |

## Killer #5: METABOLIC DERANGEMENTS

### Why RSI Kills

- **Succinylcholine raises K+ ~0.5 mEq/L**: In hyperkalemic patients, this can trigger fatal arrhythmias
- **Succinylcholine-induced cardiac arrest**: Well documented in patients with unrecognized hyperkalemia
- **Hyponatremia**: Rapid changes can cause cerebral edema or osmotic demyelination

### Which Patients

- Renal failure (especially ESRD)
- Crush injury
- Burns > 24-72 hours old
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Tumor lysis syndrome
- Addison's disease
- Spinal cord injury (chronic)
- Prolonged immobilization
- Severe hyponatremia

### Succinylcholine Contraindications

**AVOID SUCCINYLCHOLINE in:**

| Condition | Time Frame | Why |
|-----------|------------|-----|
| **Hyperkalemia (K+ > 5.5)** | Current | Additional K+ release may be fatal |
| **Burns** | > 24-72 hours post-injury | Upregulated ACh receptors |
| **Crush injury** | > 24-72 hours post-injury | Upregulated ACh receptors |
| **Spinal cord injury** | > 24-72 hours (up to 2 years) | Upregulated ACh receptors |
| **Stroke/CVA** | > 24-72 hours post-event | Upregulated ACh receptors |
| **Prolonged immobilization** | > 1 week | Upregulated ACh receptors |
| **Muscular dystrophy** | Always | Rhabdomyolysis risk |
| **Malignant hyperthermia history** | Always | MH trigger |

**Note:** Risk peaks at 7-10 days post-injury due to upregulation of extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors. Risk persists up to 2 years.

### Rocuronium as Alternative

| Agent | Dose | Onset | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|-------|
| **Rocuronium** | 1.2 mg/kg | 60 seconds | No K+ release, safe in hyperkalemia |
| **Rocuronium** | 1.6 mg/kg | 45 seconds | Higher dose for faster onset (shock patients) |

Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg has equivalent onset to succinylcholine with longer duration.

### Pre-Intubation Hyperkalemia Management

Stabilize the membrane and shift potassium BEFORE intubation:

| Intervention | Dose | Onset | Duration | Mechanism |
|--------------|------|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Calcium gluconate** | 1-2 g IV over 5-10 min | 1-3 min | 30-60 min | Membrane stabilization |
| **Calcium chloride** | 1 g IV (via central line) | 1-3 min | 30-60 min | Membrane stabilization (3x more elemental Ca) |
| **Regular insulin** | 10 units IV | 15-30 min | 4-6 hours | Intracellular K+ shift |
| **Dextrose 50%** | 50 mL (25g) IV | -- | -- | Prevent hypoglycemia with insulin |
| **Albuterol** | 10-20 mg nebulized | 15-30 min | 2-4 hours | Intracellular K+ shift |
| **Sodium bicarbonate** | 50-100 mEq IV | 30-60 min | 2-4 hours | Intracellular K+ shift (controversial) |

**Calcium first** - Stabilizes the myocardium within minutes while other therapies take effect

### Severe Hyponatremia Considerations

- **Do NOT rapidly correct** during resuscitation
- **Avoid hypotonic fluids**
- **If intubating:** Use isotonic or slightly hypertonic maintenance
- **Post-intubation:** Monitor sodium closely, correct slowly (< 8 mEq/L per 24 hours)
- **Cerebral edema risk** if hyponatremia worsens acutely

### Evidence Update (2024)

Recent retrospective study of 434 hyperkalemic ED patients:
- No significant difference in 24-hour mortality: rocuronium 10.0% vs succinylcholine 10.5% (p=0.989)
- However, this was observational with selection bias
- **Standard of care remains: Avoid succinylcholine in hyperkalemia**

## Killer #6: ELEVATED ICP

### Why RSI Kills

- **Laryngoscopy raises ICP**: Sympathetic response to airway manipulation
- **Hypotension drops CPP**: CPP = MAP - ICP. If MAP drops during induction while ICP is elevated, brain perfusion suffers
- **Hypoxia worsens secondary injury**: Already injured brain is exquisitely sensitive
- **Hypercarbia increases ICP**: CO2 is a cerebral vasodilator

### Which Patients

- Traumatic brain injury (TBI)
- Stroke with mass effect
- Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH)
- Brain tumor with edema
- Hydrocephalus
- Meningitis with increased ICP
- Hepatic encephalopathy with cerebral edema

### Key Physiologic Goals

| Parameter | Target | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| **SBP** | > 100 mmHg | Maintain CPP (CPP = MAP - ICP) |
| **MAP** | > 80 mmHg | Higher MAP needed with elevated ICP |
| **SpO2** | > 95% | Avoid hypoxia (secondary injury) |
| **PaCO2** | 35-40 mmHg | Avoid hypercarbia (raises ICP) |
| **Glucose** | 140-180 mg/dL | Avoid hypoglycemia and severe hyperglycemia |

### Pre-Intubation Optimization

1. **Position**: Keep patient upright until final moment, then 20-30 degree head elevation
2. **Control hypertension BEFORE intubation**: Nicardipine or clevidipine infusion
3. **Preoxygenate adequately**: Apneic oxygenation essential
4. **Prepare push-dose pressors**: To maintain MAP during induction

### Ketamine in Elevated ICP: OLD DOGMA DEBUNKED

**Traditional teaching:** Ketamine raises ICP and is contraindicated in TBI
**Current evidence:** This is NOT supported by modern studies

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| Ketamine does not increase ICP | Zeiler et al. systematic review |
| Safe in intubated OR non-intubated TBI patients | Multiple studies |
| May be neuroprotective | NMDA receptor antagonism |
| Maintains hemodynamic stability | Critical for CPP |

**2024-2025 Evidence:**
- Meta-analysis: Ketamine does NOT increase ICP (RR 0.67, 95% CI 0.45-1.01, p=0.054)
- Ketamine is gaining recognition for potential neuroprotection via NMDA antagonism
- Inhibits spreading depolarizations (contribute to secondary injury)
- Minimizes systemic inflammatory responses

### Induction Agent Selection

| Agent | Dose | Considerations |
|-------|------|----------------|
| **Etomidate** | 0.3 mg/kg | Traditional choice, minimal hemodynamic effect |
| **Ketamine** | 0.5-1 mg/kg | Safe in TBI, maintains hemodynamics |
| **Propofol** | 1-2 mg/kg | Reduces ICP but causes hypotension |
| **Propofol/Ketamine** | 75%/25% mix | Balanced approach |

**In hypotensive TBI patient:** Ketamine is preferred (maintains blood pressure, maintains CPP)

### Pretreatment Medications

**Fentanyl:**
- Dose: 3-5 mcg/kg IV, 3-5 minutes before laryngoscopy
- Blunts sympathetic response to intubation
- Risk: Hypotension (have push-dose pressors ready)

**Lidocaine:**
- Dose: 1.5 mg/kg IV
- Evidence for benefit is LIMITED
- Topical application more effective than IV
- Risk of hypotension documented
- **Not routinely recommended**

### Paralytic Selection

Both rocuronium and succinylcholine acceptable:
- Succinylcholine: Transient ICP rise is NOT clinically significant
- Rocuronium: Preferred if sugammadex available for reversal

### Post-Intubation Management

| Parameter | Target | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Head of bed** | 30-45 degrees | Promotes venous drainage |
| **SpO2** | > 95% | Avoid hypoxia |
| **PEEP** | Minimize (only as needed) | High PEEP can impair venous drainage |
| **ETCO2** | 35 mmHg | Then get ABG for PaCO2 |
| **PaCO2** | 35-40 mmHg | AVOID hyperventilation unless herniating |
| **Sedation** | Propofol + fentanyl | Propofol reduces CMRO2 |

### Hyperventilation

**AVOID routine hyperventilation** - Causes cerebral vasoconstriction, may worsen ischemia

**Exception: Signs of active herniation:**
- Unilateral or bilateral pupil dilation
- Posturing
- Cushing's triad (HTN, bradycardia, irregular respirations)

**If herniating:** Temporarily hyperventilate to PaCO2 30-35 mmHg while initiating other interventions (mannitol, hypertonic saline, neurosurgery consult)

## Multiple Simultaneous Killers

Many critically ill patients have multiple physiologic derangements. The approach must address ALL killers present.

### Common Combinations

| Scenario | Killers Present | Key Modifications |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Septic shock + ARDS** | Hypotension + Hypoxia | ROCKETamine + DSI + push pressors |
| **DKA + respiratory failure** | Acidosis + Hypoxia | VAPOX + high RR vent settings |
| **Massive PE** | Hypoxia + RV failure + Hypotension | Awake intubation preferred, pressors, avoid PPV |
| **TBI + hemorrhagic shock** | Elevated ICP + Hypotension | Ketamine, aggressive BP support, target CPP |
| **Renal failure + pneumonia** | Metabolic (K+) + Hypoxia + Acidosis | Rocuronium (not sux), VAPOX, treat K+ first |

### Prioritization Framework

1. **Immediate life threats first**: Airway obstruction, active hemorrhage
2. **Stabilize hemodynamics**: Push pressors, volume if indicated
3. **Optimize oxygenation**: NIV, DSI if needed
4. **Address metabolic derangements**: Calcium for hyperkalemia
5. **Then proceed to intubation**: With appropriate modifications

### The Modified RSI Approach

When multiple killers are present, standard RSI must be abandoned:

| Standard RSI | Modified RSI |
|--------------|--------------|
| Simultaneous induction + paralytic | May give induction THEN paralytic |
| No ventilation during apnea | May ventilate via NIV mask during onset |
| Standard drug doses | Reduced sedative, increased paralytic |
| Standard vent settings | Condition-specific vent settings pre-programmed |
| Wait until stable to intubate | Actively resuscitate during intubation process |

## Push-Dose Pressors Quick Reference

### Epinephrine (Preferred)

**Why:** Both alpha (vasoconstriction) AND beta (inotropy) effects

**Preparation:**
1. Draw 1 mL from cardiac epinephrine amp (1:10,000 = 100 mcg/mL)
2. Add to 9 mL normal saline in 10 mL syringe
3. Mix thoroughly (roll between palms)
4. Final concentration: **10 mcg/mL**

**Dosing:** 0.5-2 mL (5-20 mcg) IV push every 2-5 minutes

**Prophylactic dose with induction:** 5 mcg (0.5 mL) pushed with sedative

**Onset:** < 1 minute
**Duration:** ~5 minutes (up to 10)

### Phenylephrine

**Why:** Pure alpha agonist when tachycardia is concern

**Preparation:**
1. Draw 1 mL from phenylephrine vial (10 mg/mL)
2. Add to 99 mL normal saline
3. Final concentration: **100 mcg/mL**

**Dosing:** 50-200 mcg (0.5-2 mL) IV push every 2-5 minutes

**Note:** May slow onset of sedatives compared to epinephrine

### Safety Considerations

- **NEVER give code-dose epinephrine (1 mg) to patients with a pulse**
- Prefilled 10 mcg/mL syringes preferred if available
- Dosing errors common in stress situations
- Label syringe clearly

### When to Use

| Situation | Agent | Reason |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| **Peri-intubation support** | Epinephrine | Rapid onset, dual mechanism |
| **Bridge to infusion** | Epinephrine | Buys time while mixing drip |
| **Bradycardic patient** | Epinephrine | Beta effect |
| **Tachycardic patient** | Phenylephrine | Pure alpha, may slow HR |

## Apneic Oxygenation Setup

### Standard Setup (All Intubations)

1. **Nasal cannula at 15 lpm** - Leave in place during laryngoscopy
2. This is the single most important intervention

### For Hypoxemic Patients

| Equipment | Setting | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Nasal cannula** | 15 lpm | Leave in place during laryngoscopy |
| **Plus NRB** | 15 lpm | For preoxygenation |
| **Or HFNC** | 60 lpm, 100% FiO2 | If available; longer safe apnea time |
| **Or BVM + PEEP valve** | 5-15 cmH2O | For patients needing PEEP |

### HFNC vs Standard NC Evidence

| Outcome | HFNC 60 lpm | NC 15 lpm | Significance |
|---------|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| Prevention of desaturation | Similar | Similar | No superiority |
| Safe apnea time | 11 minutes | 7 minutes | HFNC longer |
| Post-intubation PaO2 | Higher | Lower | HFNC better |

**Bottom line:** HFNC provides longer safe apnea time but no difference in preventing desaturation. Standard NC at 15 lpm is adequate if HFNC unavailable.

### Positioning

- **Head elevated 20-30 degrees**
- **Ramped position** for obese patients (ear-to-sternal notch alignment)
- **Head-elevated laryngoscopy** reduces complications

## Post-Intubation Ventilator Settings

### Condition-Specific Settings

| Condition | Mode | RR | TV | PEEP | FiO2 | Target |
|-----------|------|----|----|------|------|--------|
| **Hypotension/Shock** | AC/VC | 14-16 | 6-8 mL/kg | 5 | 100% | Adequate oxygenation, minimize mean airway pressure |
| **Severe Hypoxemia** | AC/VC | 20-24 | 6 mL/kg | 10-15 | 100% | SpO2 > 90%, Pplat < 30 |
| **Severe Acidosis** | AC/VC | **30** | 8 mL/kg | 5 | 100% | Match pre-intubation ETCO2 |
| **RV Failure/PE** | AC/VC | 16-20 | 6-8 mL/kg | **5-8** | 100% | Low PEEP, avoid hypercarbia |
| **Elevated ICP** | AC/VC | 16-20 | 6-8 mL/kg | **5** (minimize) | 100% | PaCO2 35-40, SpO2 > 95% |

### Key Principles

1. **Severe acidosis:** RR 30, match pre-intubation minute ventilation
2. **RV failure:** Low PEEP, avoid hypercarbia
3. **Elevated ICP:** PaCO2 35-40 (NOT hyperventilation unless herniating)
4. **Shock:** Minimize mean airway pressure

### Get ABG Within 15 Minutes

Do not rely solely on ETCO2. Verify PaCO2 with blood gas and adjust ventilator accordingly.

## Summary: The Pre-Intubation Checklist

Before ANY high-risk intubation, systematically assess:

| Killer | Screen | If Present |
|--------|--------|------------|
| **Hypotension** | SBP < 100? On pressors? | ROCKETamine + push pressors |
| **Hypoxemia** | SpO2 < 93%? | DSI + apneic oxygenation |
| **Acidosis** | pH < 7.2? | VAPOX + high RR vent settings |
| **RV Failure** | JVD? PE? Pulm HTN? | Awake intubation, low PEEP |
| **Metabolic** | K+ > 5.5? | Rocuronium only, treat K+ first |
| **Elevated ICP** | TBI? Herniation signs? | Maintain MAP, ketamine OK, PaCO2 35-40 |

**Then:** Optimize, preoxygenate, modified RSI with condition-specific drugs/doses, post-intubation management tailored to physiology.
