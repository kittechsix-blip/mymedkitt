## 1. Definition and Diagnosis

### Modern Tissue-Based Definition (AHA 2009, reaffirmed 2023)

**TIA is now defined as:**
- An acute neurovascular syndrome with transient focal neurologic dysfunction
- Symptoms attributable to a specific vascular territory
- Rapid resolution of symptoms
- **No evidence of tissue infarction on diffusion-weighted MRI (DWI)**

**Critical Distinction:**
- Resolved symptoms + Positive DWI = **Ischemic Stroke** (not TIA)
- Resolved symptoms + Negative DWI = **True TIA**

### Time-Based vs Tissue-Based

| Approach | Definition | Limitation |
|----------|------------|------------|
| **Time-based (legacy)** | Symptoms resolve within 24 hours | ~30-50% of "TIAs" have infarct on MRI |
| **Tissue-based (current)** | No infarction on imaging regardless of duration | Requires MRI availability |

**Clinical Reality:** Most TIAs last <1 hour, often <15 minutes. If symptoms last >1 hour, likelihood of infarct on MRI increases significantly.

### Epidemiology

- **Incidence:** 240,000-500,000/year in the US
- **Increases with age:** 0.22/1000 person-years (age 45-54) to 4.88/1000 (age 85-94)
- **90-day stroke risk:** Up to 17.8%, with ~50% occurring within first 2 days
- **2-day stroke risk:** 4-8% depending on risk factors
- **7-day stroke risk:** 5-12% depending on risk factors

## 2. TIA Mimics

### Common TIA Mimics (Up to 60% of TIA clinic referrals)

| Mimic | Key Differentiating Features |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Migraine with aura** | Gradual spread of symptoms over 5-20 min, positive phenomena (scintillations, tingling), headache follows, longer duration (10-30 min typical) |
| **Seizure/Todd's paralysis** | Convulsive activity, lateral tongue bite, muscle soreness, gradual recovery over hours, may have positive phenomena |
| **Hypoglycemia** | Readily correctable, often with autonomic symptoms (sweating, tremor), altered consciousness more common |
| **Syncope** | Loss of consciousness, diffuse symptoms (not focal), prodrome (nausea, diaphoresis, pallor), rapid recovery |
| **Peripheral vestibular** | Isolated vertigo without other neurologic signs, positional component, Dix-Hallpike positive |
| **Transient global amnesia** | Isolated memory dysfunction, no motor/sensory deficits, repetitive questioning |
| **Anxiety/panic** | Bilateral symptoms, hyperventilation, non-anatomic distribution, prolonged duration |
| **Metabolic (hyponatremia, uremia)** | Global dysfunction, gradual onset, lab abnormalities |

### Red Flags FOR TIA (Against Mimics)

- **Sudden onset** (maximal at onset, "like a switch")
- **Definite focal symptoms** attributable to a single vascular territory
- **Negative symptoms** (weakness, numbness, vision loss) rather than positive
- **Speech disturbance** (aphasia, dysarthria)
- **Unilateral weakness**
- **Duration <1 hour** (most TIAs <15 minutes)
- **Vascular risk factors** present

### Red Flags AGAINST TIA (Suggest Mimic)

- Gradual onset or progression
- Positive phenomena (tingling that spreads, visual scintillations)
- Symptoms that spread from one domain to another
- Loss of consciousness (very rare in TIA)
- Bilateral symptoms
- Headache preceding symptoms
- Duration >1 hour without imaging evidence of infarct
- Symptoms not fitting a single vascular territory

## 3. Risk Stratification

### ABCD2 Score (Classic but Limited)

**Components:**

| Variable | Criteria | Points |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **A**ge | >=60 years | 1 |
| **B**lood Pressure | SBP >=140 or DBP >=90 | 1 |
| **C**linical Features | Unilateral weakness | 2 |
| | Speech disturbance without weakness | 1 |
| | Other | 0 |
| **D**uration | >=60 minutes | 2 |
| | 10-59 minutes | 1 |
| | <10 minutes | 0 |
| **D**iabetes | Present | 1 |

**Total: 0-7 points**

**2-Day Stroke Risk by ABCD2:**

| Score | Risk Category | 2-Day Stroke Risk | 7-Day Stroke Risk |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 0-3 | Low | ~1% | ~1.2% |
| 4-5 | Moderate | ~4% | ~5.9% |
| 6-7 | High | ~8% | ~11.7% |

**ABCD2 Limitations (ACEP 2016 - Level B):**
- **Do NOT rely on ABCD2 alone** to identify patients who can be safely discharged
- Poor sensitivity for identifying low-risk patients
- Poor specificity for identifying high-risk patients
- Does not account for mechanism (large vessel, cardioembolic)
- Performs worse when used by non-specialists

---

### Canadian TIA Score (Preferred - Validated 2021)

**13 Variables - Use online calculator or app**

**Clinical Variables (9):**

| Variable | Points |
|----------|--------|
| First TIA ever (vs prior TIA/stroke) | +2.3 |
| Symptom duration >=10 minutes | +2.0 |
| History of carotid stenosis | +2.0 |
| Already on antiplatelet therapy | +1.5 |
| Gait disturbance | +1.3 |
| Unilateral weakness | +1.2 |
| History of vertigo | -0.9 |
| DBP >=110 mmHg | +0.6 |
| Dysarthria or aphasia | +0.8 |

**Investigation Variables (4):**

| Variable | Points |
|----------|--------|
| Atrial fibrillation on ECG | +1.2 |
| Infarction on CT head | +1.5 |
| Platelet count >=400 x10^9/L | +1.5 |
| Glucose >=15 mmol/L (270 mg/dL) | +1.0 |

**Risk Stratification (7-Day Stroke or CEA/CAS):**

| Risk Category | Score Range | 7-Day Risk |
|---------------|-------------|------------|
| Minimal | <-1 | <0.5% |
| Low | -1 to 3 | 0.5-2.5% |
| Medium | 4-7 | 2.3% |
| High | 8-13 | 5.9% |
| Critical | >13 | >10% |

**Validation:** AUC 0.70 vs ABCD2 0.60 (p<0.001) in 7,607 patients across 13 Canadian EDs

---

### High-Risk Features (Independent of Score)

**Admit regardless of risk score if ANY present:**

1. **Crescendo TIA** (>1 TIA within 7 days)
2. **Large vessel stenosis >=50%** (carotid or intracranial)
3. **Atrial fibrillation** (new or known, needs anticoagulation)
4. **Acute infarct on imaging** (technically a stroke, not TIA)
5. **Known hypercoagulable state**
6. **Cardioembolic source** (LV thrombus, valvular disease)
7. **Stuttering/fluctuating symptoms**
8. **Cannot complete outpatient workup within 48-72 hours**

## 4. ED Workup

### Immediate Assessment

**STAT (within minutes):**
- **Fingerstick glucose** - Rule out hypoglycemia
- **Vital signs** including BP in both arms
- **Focused neuro exam** (document any persistent deficits)
- **12-lead ECG** - Detect atrial fibrillation (found in ~7% of TIA/stroke)

### Laboratory Studies

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| **Fingerstick glucose** | Hypoglycemia mimic |
| **CBC** | Anemia, thrombocytosis, polycythemia |
| **BMP/CMP** | Electrolytes, glucose, renal function for contrast |
| **Coagulation (PT/INR, PTT)** | Baseline before anticoagulation, bleeding risk |
| **Lipid panel** | Baseline, guides statin therapy |
| **HbA1c** | Undiagnosed diabetes |
| **Troponin** | Consider if cardiac symptoms or concern for MI |
| **TSH** | Consider if atrial fibrillation detected |

### Brain Imaging

**Non-Contrast CT Head:**
- **Role:** Rule out hemorrhage, mass lesion, large established stroke
- **Limitation:** Low sensitivity for acute ischemic infarct (~16-30%)
- **Obtain:** Immediately in ED if available

**MRI with DWI (Preferred):**
- **Role:** Distinguish true TIA (negative) from minor stroke (positive DWI)
- **Sensitivity:** ~80-90% for acute infarct in first 24 hours
- **Timing:** Ideally within 24 hours of symptom onset
- **Positive DWI:** ~30-50% of clinical "TIAs" have infarct = stroke, higher risk

**AHA 2023 Statement:** "When MRI cannot be obtained acutely to definitively distinguish TIA from stroke, it remains reasonable to make a clinical diagnosis of TIA in the ED on the basis of a negative NCCT and symptom resolution within 24 hours."

### Vascular Imaging

**CTA Head and Neck (Preferred in ED):**
- **Role:** Identify large vessel stenosis, occlusion, dissection
- **Findings:** ~50% of TIA patients have abnormal cerebrovascular imaging
- **Target:** Carotid stenosis >=50%, intracranial stenosis, dissection

**Alternatives:**
- **Carotid Doppler US:** Good for extracranial carotid, limited for intracranial
- **MRA:** Alternative if CTA contraindicated, may overestimate stenosis
- **Conventional angiography:** Gold standard, reserved for discordant results

**ACEP 2016 Recommendations:**
- Level B: Obtain MRI with DWI when feasible to identify high-risk patients
- Level B: Obtain cervical vascular imaging (CTA, MRA, or US) to identify high-risk patients

### Cardiac Workup

**ECG (Required):**
- Detect atrial fibrillation/flutter
- Evidence of prior MI, LVH
- Obtain ASAP, ideally before disposition

**Echocardiography:**

| Type | When to Order | What It Detects |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| **TTE** | All patients eventually | LV thrombus, valvular disease, PFO (limited), wall motion abnormalities |
| **TEE** | Suspected cardioembolic, no clear etiology, young patient | PFO, atrial septal aneurysm, left atrial appendage thrombus, aortic arch atheroma |
| **Bubble study** | Cryptogenic stroke in younger patient | Right-to-left shunt (PFO) |

**Can often be deferred to outpatient if low-risk features and rapid follow-up available**

### Cardiac Rhythm Monitoring

**For Occult Atrial Fibrillation:**
- **24-48 hour Holter:** If high clinical suspicion, can detect paroxysmal AF
- **Extended monitoring (14-30 days):** If cryptogenic, higher yield
- **Implantable loop recorder:** Highest yield for cryptogenic stroke

**Yield:** AF detected in additional 5-15% with extended monitoring vs ED ECG alone

## 5. Etiology Workup

### TOAST Classification (Stroke Etiology)

| Category | Prevalence | Key Features | Workup |
|----------|------------|--------------|--------|
| **Large artery atherosclerosis** | 15-25% | Carotid stenosis >=50%, intracranial stenosis | CTA/MRA, carotid US |
| **Cardioembolism** | 20-30% | Afib, LV thrombus, valve disease, recent MI | ECG, echo, monitoring |
| **Small vessel disease (lacunar)** | 20-25% | HTN, DM, typical lacunar syndromes, subcortical location | MRI, risk factor assessment |
| **Other determined** | 5% | Dissection, hypercoagulable, vasculitis, RCVS | CTA, coag workup, CSF |
| **Cryptogenic** | 25-40% | No clear etiology after workup | Extended monitoring, PFO evaluation |

### Large Artery Atherosclerosis

**Carotid Stenosis Assessment:**

| Stenosis Grade | Management |
|----------------|------------|
| <50% | Medical management |
| 50-69% | Consider CEA/CAS, medical management reasonable |
| >=70% | CEA/CAS recommended if life expectancy >5 years |

**NASCET criteria for measuring stenosis on angiography/CTA**

**Intervention Timing:**
- **Within 2 weeks** of TIA/minor stroke for symptomatic stenosis >=50%
- **CEA preferred over CAS** when performed within 7 days (lower stroke risk)
- NNT = 3 if CEA within 2 weeks, NNT = 6 if delayed 2-4 weeks

### Cardioembolism

**Atrial Fibrillation:**
- Most common cardioembolic source
- Requires anticoagulation (see Treatment section)
- If not detected on initial ECG, consider extended monitoring

**Other Sources:**
- LV thrombus (recent MI, cardiomyopathy)
- Mechanical valve
- Infective endocarditis
- Left atrial appendage thrombus
- Atrial myxoma

### Patent Foramen Ovale (PFO)

**When to Evaluate:**
- Cryptogenic stroke/TIA
- Age <60 years (primary guideline recommendation)
- Consider in older patients with high-risk PFO features

**High-Risk PFO Features:**
- Atrial septal aneurysm
- Large shunt (>20 microbubbles)
- Hypermobile septum

**PFO Closure Evidence (REDUCE, CLOSE, DEFENSE-PFO trials):**
- HR 0.41 for stroke recurrence vs medical therapy
- ARR 3.4% at 5 years
- Increased atrial fibrillation risk (generally transient)
- Greatest benefit in patients <60 with high-risk features

### Dissection Workup

**When to Suspect:**
- Younger patient
- Recent trauma or neck manipulation
- Neck pain or headache
- Horner's syndrome (carotid)
- CN deficits (vertebral)

**Imaging:** CTA or MRA neck with fat-saturated sequences

### Hypercoagulable Workup

**When to Consider:**
- Young patient (<55) without traditional risk factors
- History of VTE or recurrent pregnancy loss
- Family history of thrombosis
- Cryptogenic after routine workup

**Tests:** Factor V Leiden, Prothrombin G20210A, Protein C/S, Antithrombin III, Antiphospholipid antibodies

## 6. Treatment

### Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)

**Indication:** Minor non-cardioembolic ischemic stroke (NIHSS <=3) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 >=4)

**Based on CHANCE and POINT Trials**

#### CHANCE Trial (Chinese population)
- Aspirin 75 mg + Clopidogrel 300 mg load, then 75 mg daily x 21 days
- Then aspirin monotherapy
- **Result:** 32% relative risk reduction in stroke at 90 days
- No increase in major bleeding

#### POINT Trial (International)
- Aspirin 50-325 mg + Clopidogrel 600 mg load, then 75 mg daily x 90 days
- **Result:** 28% reduction in major ischemic events
- Increased major bleeding (0.9% vs 0.4%) mostly after day 21

**Current Recommendation (AHA/ASA 2021 - Class I):**

```
DAPT PROTOCOL FOR HIGH-RISK TIA OR MINOR STROKE

LOADING:
- Aspirin 162-325 mg (if not already on aspirin)
- Clopidogrel 300-600 mg loading dose

MAINTENANCE:
- Aspirin 81 mg daily + Clopidogrel 75 mg daily

DURATION:
- 21 days of DAPT (optimal based on pooled analysis)
- Then transition to aspirin monotherapy

TIMING:
- Ideally within 12-24 hours of symptom onset
- May consider up to 7 days after onset
```

**Who NOT to Give DAPT:**
- Cardioembolic TIA (needs anticoagulation instead)
- Moderate-severe stroke (NIHSS >3) - no benefit, more bleeding
- High bleeding risk
- On anticoagulation

### Anticoagulation

**For Atrial Fibrillation:**

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| **Apixaban** | 5 mg BID (2.5 mg if >=2: age >=80, weight <=60 kg, Cr >=1.5) | Preferred - lowest bleeding risk |
| **Rivaroxaban** | 20 mg daily with food (15 mg if CrCl 15-50) | Once daily dosing |
| **Dabigatran** | 150 mg BID (75 mg if CrCl 15-30) | Reversible with idarucizumab |
| **Edoxaban** | 60 mg daily (30 mg if CrCl 15-50, weight <=60 kg) | Not first-line if CrCl >95 |
| **Warfarin** | Target INR 2-3 | If DOAC contraindicated, valvular AF, mechanical valve |

**Timing of Anticoagulation Initiation:**
- TIA: Can start immediately (no bleeding risk)
- Minor stroke: 1-3 days
- Moderate stroke: 3-6 days
- Large stroke: 12-14 days

**"1-2-3-4 Day Rule" for DOACs after stroke:**
- Day 1: TIA (no infarct)
- Day 2-3: Minor stroke (small infarct)
- Day 3-4: Moderate stroke
- Day 12+: Large stroke or hemorrhagic transformation

### High-Intensity Statin

**Based on SPARCL Trial:**
- Atorvastatin 80 mg reduced stroke recurrence by 16%
- Reduced fatal stroke by 43%
- Slight increase in hemorrhagic stroke (not clinically significant)

**Recommendation:**

```
STATIN PROTOCOL

PREFERRED:
- Atorvastatin 40-80 mg daily
- OR Rosuvastatin 20-40 mg daily

TARGET:
- LDL <70 mg/dL (some guidelines <55 mg/dL for very high risk)
- OR >=50% reduction from baseline

TIMING:
- Start immediately in ED if not contraindicated
- Do not wait for lipid panel results
```

### Blood Pressure Management

**Acute Phase (First 24-48 hours):**
- **Permissive hypertension** - Do not aggressively lower unless:
  - SBP >220 mmHg or DBP >120 mmHg
  - Hypertensive emergency with end-organ damage
  - Receiving thrombolytics (target <185/110)

**Long-Term Target:**
- <130/80 mmHg (AHA/ASA 2021)
- Initiate/resume antihypertensives before discharge
- ACE inhibitor or ARB + thiazide diuretic often preferred

### Glucose Management

- Target blood glucose 140-180 mg/dL acutely
- Avoid hypoglycemia
- HbA1c target <7% long-term for most patients
- Address undiagnosed diabetes

### Carotid Intervention

**Carotid Endarterectomy (CEA) vs Carotid Artery Stenting (CAS):**

| Feature | CEA | CAS |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Preferred if** | Surgery within 2 weeks, age >70, anatomy favorable | High surgical risk, radiation to neck, prior CEA |
| **Timing** | Within 2 weeks of event | Within 2 weeks if CEA contraindicated |
| **Early stroke risk** | 1.4% if within 2 weeks | Higher than CEA in first week (8.3% vs 1.3%) |

**Indications for Intervention:**
- Symptomatic carotid stenosis >=50-99%
- Within 2 weeks of TIA/minor stroke
- Life expectancy >5 years
- Perioperative risk <6%

**Contraindications to Early Revascularization:**
- Disabling stroke (mRS >=3)
- Large infarct (>30% MCA territory)
- Altered consciousness

## 7. Disposition

### Admission Criteria

**Admit if ANY of the following:**

1. **High-risk features:**
   - Crescendo TIA
   - Atrial fibrillation requiring anticoagulation initiation
   - Symptomatic carotid stenosis >=50%
   - Acute infarct on DWI
   - Known hypercoagulable state
   - Cardioembolic source identified

2. **Clinical instability:**
   - Fluctuating or recurrent symptoms
   - Hypertensive emergency
   - Medical comorbidities requiring inpatient management
   - New onset atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response

3. **Cannot complete outpatient workup:**
   - No rapid TIA clinic access (<48-72 hours)
   - Patient unable to return promptly
   - ABCD2 score 2-7 AND workup cannot be completed within 7 days

### ED Observation Unit Criteria

**Consider 23-hour observation if:**
- Moderate risk (ABCD2 3-5 or Canadian TIA medium risk)
- All imaging/workup can be completed in observation period
- Neurology consultation available
- No high-risk features

**Observation Unit Protocol:**
- Complete CTA head/neck
- MRI brain with DWI if available
- Continuous telemetry monitoring
- Echo (TTE)
- Neurology consultation
- Initiate secondary prevention

### Discharge Criteria

**May discharge from ED if ALL of the following:**

1. Low-risk score (ABCD2 0-2 AND Canadian TIA minimal/low risk)
2. **AND** no high-risk features on history/exam
3. **AND** CT head negative for hemorrhage/mass
4. **AND** CTA shows no significant stenosis (<50%)
5. **AND** ECG shows no atrial fibrillation
6. **AND** reliable rapid outpatient follow-up within 24-48 hours
7. **AND** patient can recognize warning signs and return immediately
8. **AND** secondary prevention initiated (aspirin/DAPT, statin)

### Outpatient Rapid TIA Clinic

**Requirements for Safe Outpatient Management:**

```
RAPID TIA CLINIC ELEMENTS

TIMING: Seen within 24-48 hours (ideally <24 hours)

WORKUP COMPLETED:
- MRI brain with DWI
- Vascular imaging (if not done in ED)
- Echocardiogram (TTE, consider TEE if indicated)
- Extended cardiac monitoring if no AF on ECG
- Hypercoagulable workup if indicated

TREATMENT INITIATED:
- DAPT or anticoagulation (per etiology)
- High-intensity statin
- BP management plan
- Risk factor modification

FOLLOW-UP ARRANGED:
- Neurology follow-up
- Vascular surgery if significant stenosis
- Cardiology if cardiac source
- PCP for risk factor management
```

### When to Call Neurology

**Consult in ED:**
- Uncertain diagnosis (TIA vs mimic)
- Fluctuating or recurrent symptoms
- Significant large vessel stenosis
- Consideration for thrombolysis (if symptoms ongoing)
- Any admission consideration

**Arrange Outpatient Follow-up:**
- All TIA patients should see neurology within 48 hours (ideally) to 1 week
- Sooner if any concerning features

### Stroke Center Transfer Criteria

**Consider transfer to Comprehensive Stroke Center if:**
- Symptomatic carotid stenosis requiring urgent intervention (within 48 hours)
- Consideration for endovascular intervention
- Need for TEE or advanced cardiac workup not available
- Neurology expertise not available locally
- Complex etiology requiring subspecialty evaluation

## 9. Key Trials

### CHANCE Trial (2013)
- **Population:** 5,170 Chinese patients with minor stroke or high-risk TIA
- **Intervention:** Aspirin + Clopidogrel 300 mg load, then 75 mg x 21 days vs aspirin alone
- **Result:** 32% RRR in stroke at 90 days (8.2% vs 11.7%)
- **Bleeding:** No significant difference
- **Conclusion:** DAPT for 21 days is safe and effective

### POINT Trial (2018)
- **Population:** 4,881 international patients with minor stroke or high-risk TIA
- **Intervention:** Aspirin + Clopidogrel 600 mg load, then 75 mg x 90 days vs aspirin alone
- **Result:** 28% RRR in major ischemic events
- **Bleeding:** Increased after day 21 (0.9% vs 0.4%)
- **Conclusion:** DAPT benefit in first 21 days, excess bleeding after

### EXPRESS Trial (2007)
- **Population:** 591 UK patients with TIA or minor stroke
- **Intervention:** Rapid assessment (<1 day) vs delayed (3 days)
- **Result:** 80% reduction in 90-day stroke risk (2.1% vs 10.3%)
- **Conclusion:** Urgent assessment and treatment dramatically reduces stroke risk

### SPARCL Trial (2006)
- **Population:** 4,731 patients with recent stroke or TIA
- **Intervention:** Atorvastatin 80 mg vs placebo
- **Result:** 16% RRR in stroke, 43% RRR in fatal stroke
- **Conclusion:** High-intensity statin reduces stroke recurrence

### NASCET/ECST (1991/1998)
- **Finding:** CEA reduces stroke risk in symptomatic carotid stenosis >=70%
- **Timing:** Benefit greatest when performed within 2 weeks

### PFO Closure Trials (REDUCE, CLOSE, DEFENSE-PFO)
- **Population:** Cryptogenic stroke with PFO, mostly age <60
- **Result:** HR 0.41 for stroke recurrence with closure vs medical therapy
- **Conclusion:** PFO closure reduces recurrent stroke in selected patients
