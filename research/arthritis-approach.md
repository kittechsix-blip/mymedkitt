# Arthritis Approach: Undifferentiated Joint Pain

**Research compiled:** 2026-04-05
**Category:** Rheumatology
**Tree ID:** `arthritis-approach`
**Purpose:** Hub consult for differentiating causes of joint pain; links to specific consults (gout, septic arthritis)

---

## 1. Diagnostic Framework Overview

### Joint Distribution Classification

| Classification | Definition | Common Causes |
|----------------|------------|---------------|
| **Monoarticular** | Single joint involved | Septic arthritis, gout, pseudogout, trauma, hemarthrosis, Lyme |
| **Oligoarticular** | 2-4 joints involved | Reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, early RA, DGI |
| **Polyarticular** | 5+ joints involved | RA, SLE, viral arthritis, OA (generalized) |

**Key insight:** Many polyarticular conditions initially present as monoarthritis. Always consider the possibility that monoarthritis may be the first manifestation of a systemic disease.

### Timeline Classification

| Timeline | Definition | Common Causes |
|----------|------------|---------------|
| **Hyperacute** (seconds-minutes) | Trauma, fracture, dislocation, loose body |
| **Acute** (<6 weeks) | Infection, crystal arthropathy, reactive arthritis, viral |
| **Chronic** (>6 weeks) | RA, OA, psoriatic arthritis, SLE |

### Six Key Clinical Factors (Polyarticular Framework)

1. **Disease chronology** - Acute vs chronic
2. **Inflammation** - Present or absent
3. **Distribution** - Symmetric vs asymmetric; large vs small joints
4. **Extra-articular manifestations** - Skin, eyes, GU symptoms
5. **Disease course** - Additive, migratory, intermittent
6. **Patient demographics** - Age, sex, risk factors

## 2. Inflammatory vs Mechanical (Degenerative)

### Key Distinguishing Features

| Feature | Inflammatory | Mechanical/Degenerative |
|---------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Morning stiffness** | >60 minutes (often hours) | <30 minutes |
| **Response to rest** | Worsens with inactivity | Improves with rest |
| **Response to activity** | Improves with movement | Worsens with activity |
| **Joint appearance** | Warm, swollen, erythematous | Bony enlargement, minimal warmth |
| **Systemic symptoms** | Fatigue, fever, weight loss | Absent |
| **Night pain** | Common | Uncommon (unless severe OA) |
| **Pattern** | Often symmetric | Often asymmetric, DIP involvement |

### ASAS Criteria for Inflammatory Back Pain (4/5 = positive)

1. Age at onset <40 years
2. Insidious onset
3. Improvement with exercise
4. No improvement with rest
5. Pain at night (improving on getting up)

**Sensitivity:** 77% | **Specificity:** 91.7%

## 3. Septic Arthritis

### Red Flags - MUST NOT MISS

- Fever (though 77% are afebrile in some studies)
- Single hot, swollen joint
- Inability to bear weight
- Recent joint surgery or injection
- Prosthetic joint
- Immunocompromised state
- IV drug use
- Overlying cellulitis
- Rapid symptom progression

### Kocher Criteria (Pediatric Hip - NOT validated for adults)

| Criteria Present | Probability of Septic Arthritis |
|------------------|--------------------------------|
| 0 | 2% |
| 1 | 9.5% |
| 2 | 35% |
| 3 | 72.8% |
| 4 | 93% |

**The 4 Kocher criteria:**
1. Fever >38.5C
2. Non-weight bearing
3. WBC >12,000/mm3
4. ESR >/=40 mm/h

**Modified Kocher (Caird 2006):** Adds CRP >2.0 mg/dL as 5th criterion - 98% PPV when all 5 present.

### Adult Septic Arthritis - Key Points

**Kocher criteria have LIMITED utility in adults:**
- 64% of adult septic arthritis patients have NORMAL serum WBC
- 77% are AFEBRILE
- ESR and serum WBC not statistically different between septic and non-septic groups

**Best predictors in adults:**
- Recent joint surgery
- Cellulitis overlying prosthetic joint
- Synovial WBC count (most important single test)

### Septic Arthritis Score (SAS) - Novel Adult Tool

**Variables:**
1. Synovial-to-serum glucose quotient
2. Synovial WBC count
3. Abnormal synovial fluid appearance
4. Triage priority

**Performance:** AUC 0.971 | 92% sensitivity and specificity at 10% probability threshold

**Calculators:**
- SAS: http://sascore.org (not yet externally validated)
- SARC: septicknee.org (AUC 0.89 in validation)

## 4. Synovial Fluid Analysis

### WBC Count Interpretation

| Classification | WBC/mm3 | PMN % | Likely Etiology |
|----------------|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Non-inflammatory** | 200-2,000 | <25% | OA, trauma |
| **Inflammatory** | 2,000-50,000 | 50-70% | RA, crystal, reactive |
| **Infectious** | >50,000 | >75% | Septic arthritis |
| **Highly suspicious for septic** | >85,000 | >90% | High specificity for infection |

### Critical Caveats

1. **The 50,000 cutoff is INSENSITIVE:** 39% of culture-positive septic joints have WBC <50,000
2. **Prior antibiotics lower the threshold:** Optimal cutoff drops to >16,000 if antibiotics given
3. **PMN >90% increases likelihood** of septic arthritis (LR+ 3.4)
4. **Crystals do NOT rule out infection:** 0.8% have concomitant crystal disease + septic arthritis
5. **Gram stain sensitivity only 29-50%**
6. **Culture sensitivity only 82%**

### Synovial Fluid Quick Reference

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Needle-shaped, negative birefringent crystals** | Gout (MSU) |
| **Rhomboid, positive birefringent crystals** | Pseudogout (CPPD) |
| **Bloody** | Trauma, hemophilia, tumor, over-anticoagulation |
| **Lipid droplets** | Fracture (fat from marrow) |
| **Low glucose (synovial:serum <0.5)** | Infection or RA |

## 5. Crystal Arthropathies

### Gout

**Clinical Features:**
- Sudden onset monoarthritis (often nocturnal)
- 1st MTP most common (podagra) - 50% of initial attacks
- Intense pain, erythema, warmth
- Systemic symptoms possible (fever, chills)
- Typically middle-aged men
- Tophi in chronic/advanced disease

**Diagnosis:**
- **Gold standard:** MSU crystals on polarized microscopy (needle-shaped, negative birefringent)
- Serum uric acid often NORMAL during acute attack
- 2015 ACR/EULAR criteria: >/=8 points (sensitivity 92%, specificity 89%)

**2015 ACR/EULAR Gout Classification Criteria Domains:**
1. Clinical pattern of joint involvement
2. Characteristics of episodes (max pain <24h, erythema, inability to bear touch)
3. Time course (recurrence, resolution)
4. Serum urate level
5. Imaging (double contour sign on US, urate on DECT)
6. Synovial fluid MSU crystals (sufficient criterion if positive)

**Treatment (Acute):**
- NSAIDs (indomethacin, naproxen)
- Colchicine (best within 12-24 hours of onset)
- Corticosteroids (oral, intra-articular, or IM)
- IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra) for refractory cases

### Pseudogout (CPPD)

**Clinical Features:**
- Acute attacks similar to gout ("pseudogout")
- Knee most commonly affected (vs 1st MTP in gout)
- Can present as chronic inflammatory polyarthritis mimicking RA
- "Crowned dens syndrome" - neck pain, fever mimicking meningitis
- Often associated with OA
- Lateral compartment knee involvement (vs medial in primary OA)

**Risk Factors:**
- Age >60
- Prior joint injury
- Metabolic diseases (hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis, hypomagnesemia)

**Diagnosis:**
- CPPD crystals: rhomboid-shaped, weakly positive birefringent
- Chondrocalcinosis on X-ray (calcification in fibrocartilage/hyaline cartilage)
- Common sites: knee menisci, triangular fibrocartilage of wrist, symphysis pubis

**Key Difference from Gout:**
- No disease-modifying therapy exists (cannot dissolve CPPD crystals)
- Treatment is symptomatic only

## 6. Rheumatoid Arthritis

### Clinical Features
- Symmetric polyarthritis
- Small joints of hands (MCP, PIP) and wrists most common
- Morning stiffness >1 hour
- Rheumatoid nodules
- Extra-articular: lung disease, vasculitis, eye involvement

### ED Presentation Considerations

**Acute Flare Management:**
- High-dose NSAIDs
- Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone, often 2-4 week taper)
- Failure to respond or already on steroids may require admission

**CRITICAL - Septic Arthritis Mimicry:**
- RA patients at HIGH RISK for septic arthritis
- Monoarticular or oligoarticular flare requires septic workup
- Redness over a swollen joint is UNUSUAL in RA - raises suspicion for infection
- Average delay in diagnosis: 1-3 weeks (leading to irreversible damage)
- Mortality approaches 30% with delayed diagnosis

**Special Considerations:**
- Atlantoaxial instability - prepare for difficult airway
- DMARD therapy = immunocompromised
- Up to 40% have normal ESR/CRP even during active disease

## 7. Reactive Arthritis

### Classic Triad (minority present with all 3)
1. Arthritis
2. Urethritis
3. Conjunctivitis

"Can't see, can't pee, can't climb a tree"

### Clinical Features
- Asymmetric oligoarthritis (large joints, lower extremity)
- Develops 1-4 weeks after GI or GU infection
- Dactylitis ("sausage digits")
- Enthesitis (Achilles, plantar fascia)
- Sacroiliitis (20%+)
- Keratoderma blennorrhagicum (hyperkeratotic plaques on palms/soles)
- Circinate balanitis
- Painless oral ulcers

### Triggering Infections
- **GI:** Shigella, Salmonella, Yersinia, Campylobacter
- **GU:** Chlamydia trachomatis, Ureaplasma
- **Other:** Mycobacterium, Streptococcus, SARS-CoV-2

### Diagnosis
- Clinical diagnosis (no validated criteria)
- HLA-B27 positive in ~80% (but not diagnostic)
- Synovial fluid culture NEGATIVE
- NAAT for Chlamydia/GC from urine or swab
- Diagnostic sensitivity 69%, specificity 93.5% with: GU symptoms + MTP involvement + elevated CRP + HLA-B27

### Prognosis
- Usually self-limited (3-5 months)
- 15-30% develop chronic arthritis
- Antibiotics treat infection but do NOT cure established arthritis

## 8. Osteoarthritis

### Clinical Features
- Gradual onset pain worsened by activity, relieved by rest
- Morning stiffness <30 minutes
- Bony enlargement (Heberden nodes at DIP, Bouchard nodes at PIP)
- Crepitus
- Limited ROM
- Non-inflammatory effusions

### Diagnostic Criteria (Clinical)
1. Pain worse with activity, better with rest
2. Age >45 years
3. Morning stiffness <30 minutes
4. Bony joint enlargement
5. Limited ROM

### Distribution
- Weight-bearing joints: knees, hips
- Hands: DIP > PIP > 1st CMC ("squaring" of thumb base)
- Spine (facet joints, not inflammatory)

### Key Points
- Prevalence increases with age (33.6% of adults >65)
- Radiographic changes often do NOT correlate with symptoms
- Pain mechanisms: mechanical, inflammatory, neuropathic

## 9. Gonococcal Arthritis (DGI)

### Two Presentations

**1. Arthritis-Dermatitis Syndrome (60%)**
- Migratory polyarthralgias (asymmetric, upper > lower extremity)
- Tenosynovitis
- Dermatitis (tiny pustules/vesicles on erythematous base, may become necrotic)
- Classic triad: rash + polyarthralgia + tenosynovitis
- Blood cultures positive in ~50%

**2. Purulent Monoarthritis (40%)**
- Single septic joint
- Knee, wrist, ankle most common
- Synovial fluid culture often negative

### Risk Factors
- Sexually active young adult
- Female (menstruation, pregnancy increase risk)
- MSM
- Multiple sexual partners

### Diagnosis
- NAAT from urogenital sites (most sensitive)
- Blood cultures (2 sets)
- Synovial fluid culture (often negative)
- Skin lesion culture (usually negative)
- Only 25% have genitourinary symptoms

### Key Point
Consider DGI in any sexually active patient with skin lesions + joint pain, even without classic triad.

## 10. Lyme Arthritis

### Clinical Features
- Monoarthritis or oligoarthritis
- Knee most commonly affected
- Develops weeks to months after tick bite
- Less painful than septic arthritis (patients can still ambulate)
- NO fever or prominent systemic symptoms (unlike septic)
- May have history of erythema migrans rash

### Distinguishing from Septic Arthritis

| Feature | Lyme Arthritis | Septic Arthritis |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| Fever | Absent | Often present |
| Knee involvement | Common | Less specific |
| CRP | Usually normal | Elevated |
| Synovial WBC | 10,000-50,000 | >50,000 |
| Synovial PMN% | Lower | >90% |

### Diagnosis
- Serology (2-tier testing) - excellent sensitivity in Lyme arthritis
- PCR of synovial fluid if diagnosis uncertain
- History of tick exposure (endemic area)

### Treatment
- Oral antibiotics x 4 weeks (doxycycline or amoxicillin)
- Persistent arthritis may need second course
- Delayed treatment risks permanent joint damage

## 11. Viral Arthritis

### General Features
- Acute-onset polyarticular arthritis
- Usually symmetric
- Self-limited (weeks to months)
- May have low-titer RF or ANA
- Milder than RA/OA

### Specific Viruses

**Parvovirus B19:**
- "Slapped cheek" rash in children
- RA-mimic in adults (symmetric small joint polyarthritis)
- 60% of infected adults have joint symptoms
- Usually resolves in weeks

**Hepatitis B/C:**
- Can mimic RA, SLE, vasculitis
- Check viral serologies in new polyarthritis

**HIV:**
- May be first presentation
- Can cause reactive arthritis or psoriatic arthritis

**Chikungunya:**
- Travel history to endemic area (tropics)
- Acute febrile illness with severe polyarthralgia
- Can become chronic, mimicking RA
- Mosquito-borne (Aedes)

### Key Point
Always obtain travel history in acute polyarthritis presentations.

---

## 12. SLE Arthritis

### Clinical Features
- Polyarticular arthritis in 90% of SLE patients
- Symmetric, affecting hands, wrists, knees
- Pain often disproportionate to visible swelling
- RARELY erosive or permanently deforming
- Jaccoud's arthropathy: reducible deformities from ligament laxity

### Distinguishing from RA
- Less synovitis, more tendonitis/tenosynovitis
- Non-erosive (usually)
- Associated systemic features (rash, serositis, nephritis, CNS)
- ANA positive (high sensitivity)
- Anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith specific for SLE

### AVN Risk
- Steroids + SLE = high risk for avascular necrosis (hip, knee)
- Consider in SLE patient with new hip/knee pain

---

## 13. Psoriatic Arthritis

### Clinical Features
- Associated with psoriasis (skin may precede arthritis by years)
- Asymmetric oligoarthritis (classic)
- DIP involvement (distinguishes from RA)
- Dactylitis ("sausage digits") - 16-49% of patients
- Enthesitis (Achilles, plantar fascia)
- Nail pitting and onycholysis
- Axial involvement (sacroiliitis, spondylitis)
- RF NEGATIVE

### CASPAR Classification Criteria
Entry: Inflammatory articular disease (joint, spine, or enthesis)

Then score >/=3 points from:
- Current psoriasis (2 points)
- History of psoriasis (1 point)
- Family history of psoriasis (1 point)
- Psoriatic nail dystrophy (1 point)
- Negative RF (1 point)
- Dactylitis (1 point)
- Radiographic new bone formation near joint (1 point)

### Key Imaging Finding
- "Pencil-in-cup" deformity
- New bone formation (enthesophytes)
- Periostitis

## 14. Imaging Approach

### First-Line: Plain Radiographs

**Indications:**
- All suspected arthritis (baseline)
- Trauma evaluation
- Chronic joint pain

**Findings by Condition:**
| Condition | X-ray Findings |
|-----------|----------------|
| OA | Joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis/cysts |
| RA | Periarticular osteopenia, erosions, joint space loss |
| Gout | Punched-out erosions with overhanging edges ("rat bite"), tophi |
| Pseudogout | Chondrocalcinosis |
| Psoriatic | Pencil-in-cup, periostitis, new bone formation |
| Septic | Joint effusion, soft tissue swelling (early); destruction (late) |

### Ultrasound

**Advantages:**
- Real-time imaging
- No radiation
- Can guide aspiration
- Detects synovitis, effusion, erosions, crystals
- "Double contour sign" for gout (MSU on cartilage surface)

**Indications:**
- Joint effusion evaluation
- Guided arthrocentesis
- Early RA detection
- Crystal disease confirmation

### MRI

**Advantages:**
- Best for soft tissue, bone marrow edema
- Detects early erosions before X-ray
- Can see synovitis, tenosynovitis, enthesitis

**Indications:**
- Suspected osteomyelitis
- Early inflammatory arthritis
- Spinal involvement
- AVN evaluation
- When X-ray/US non-diagnostic

**Limitations:**
- Cost, availability
- Contraindicated with some implants
- Requires gadolinium for optimal synovitis detection (renal concerns)

### CT/DECT

- Dual-energy CT can detect urate deposits (gout)
- Standard CT for bone detail, fractures

## 15. Laboratory Workup

### Inflammatory Markers

| Test | Utility | Caveats |
|------|---------|---------|
| **ESR** | Non-specific inflammation marker | Elevated with age, anemia, infection, pregnancy |
| **CRP** | Slightly more specific than ESR | Elevated with obesity, infection, smoking |

**Key point:** 60% of early RA patients have NORMAL ESR/CRP

### Autoantibodies

| Test | Target Condition | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| **RF** | RA | 50-85% | Low | Present in 1-5% of healthy people; also positive in infections, liver disease, other CTD |
| **Anti-CCP** | RA | 70% | 98% | Most specific for RA; predicts erosive disease |
| **ANA** | SLE, CTD | High for SLE | Low | ~40% of RA positive too |
| **Anti-dsDNA** | SLE | Lower | High for SLE | Specific for SLE |
| **HLA-B27** | SpA, ReA, AS | Variable | Low | Not diagnostic; supports clinical picture |

### Other Labs

| Test | When to Order | Interpretation |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| **Uric acid** | Suspected gout | Often NORMAL during acute attack; high level is risk factor but not diagnostic |
| **Lyme serology** | Endemic area, monoarthritis | 2-tier testing; excellent sensitivity in Lyme arthritis |
| **GC/CT NAAT** | Suspected DGI, young sexually active | Most sensitive test for gonococcal infection |
| **CBC** | All | Leukocytosis may suggest infection |
| **BMP** | Pre-NSAID, contrast | Renal function |
| **LFTs** | Hepatitis-associated, pre-DMARD | |
| **Viral serologies** | New polyarthritis, risk factors | Hepatitis B/C, HIV, Parvovirus IgM |

### Lab Panel by Clinical Scenario

**Monoarthritis Workup:**
- Synovial fluid analysis (cell count, crystals, Gram stain, culture)
- CBC, BMP, ESR/CRP
- Blood cultures if febrile
- Uric acid
- Consider: Lyme serology, GC/CT NAAT

**Polyarthritis Workup:**
- CBC, BMP, ESR/CRP
- RF, anti-CCP
- ANA
- Hepatitis B/C, HIV (if risk factors)
- Uric acid
- Consider: Parvovirus IgM, Lyme, HLA-B27

## 16. Calculator Concepts

### 1. Inflammatory vs Mechanical Scorer

**Purpose:** Help determine if joint symptoms are inflammatory or mechanical based on clinical features

**Variables:**
- Morning stiffness duration (<30 min = mechanical, >60 min = inflammatory)
- Response to rest (improves = mechanical, worsens = inflammatory)
- Response to activity (worsens = mechanical, improves = inflammatory)
- Joint warmth/erythema
- Systemic symptoms
- Night pain

**Output:** Probability of inflammatory vs mechanical etiology with guidance on workup

### 2. Synovial Fluid Interpreter

**Purpose:** Guide interpretation of synovial fluid results

**Inputs:**
- WBC count
- PMN percentage
- Crystal presence and type
- Gram stain result
- Glucose (synovial:serum ratio)
- Appearance (clear, cloudy, purulent, bloody)

**Outputs:**
- Probability of septic arthritis
- Likely diagnoses
- Need for empiric antibiotics
- Red flags

### 3. Septic Arthritis Risk Calculator (Based on SAS/SARC)

**Purpose:** Estimate probability of septic arthritis in native joint

**Variables:**
- Synovial WBC count
- Synovial:serum glucose ratio
- Fluid appearance
- Clinical risk factors (immunocompromised, IV drug use, recent surgery, prosthetic joint)
- Fever
- Serum WBC

**Output:** Probability percentage with management guidance

### 4. Arthritis Pattern Matcher

**Purpose:** Generate differential diagnosis based on pattern recognition

**Inputs:**
- Number of joints (1 vs 2-4 vs 5+)
- Distribution (symmetric vs asymmetric)
- Joint size (large vs small)
- Timeline (acute vs chronic)
- Inflammatory features (yes/no)
- Extra-articular features (skin, eyes, GU, GI)
- Age/sex
- Risk factors

**Output:** Ranked differential diagnosis with likelihood and recommended workup for each

### 5. Lab Panel Interpreter

**Purpose:** Interpret arthritis lab results in clinical context

**Inputs:**
- RF result
- Anti-CCP result
- ANA result/titer
- ESR
- CRP
- Uric acid
- Synovial fluid findings

**Output:**
- Probability of RA (seropositive vs seronegative)
- Probability of SLE
- Probability of gout
- Next steps
