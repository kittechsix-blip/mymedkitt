## Classification Systems

### Wagner Classification (Grades 0-5)

The Wagner Classification System (1981) uses 6 grades based on ulcer depth and presence of gangrene.

| Grade | Description | Criteria |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **0** | Pre-ulcerative | Intact skin, bony deformity, or cellulitis present |
| **1** | Superficial ulcer | Partial or full-thickness ulcer, epidermis/dermis only |
| **2** | Deep ulcer | Extends to ligament, tendon, joint capsule, or fascia (no abscess/osteo) |
| **3** | Deep ulcer + osteo | Abscess, osteomyelitis, or tendonitis present |
| **4** | Partial gangrene | Limited necrosis in toes or forefoot |
| **5** | Extensive gangrene | Necrosis of entire foot |

**Limitations:** Does not assess infection or ischemia separately. Despite this, Wagner 3+ had highest predictive value for amputation (AUC 0.892 in comparative study).

**Source:** [Wound Care Education Partners](https://www.woundeducationpartners.com/resources/rx-pad-blog/entry/part-2-evaluation-and-management-of-the-diabetic-foot-ulcer-classification-of-diabetic-foot-ulcer.html)

---

### University of Texas (UT) Classification

A 4x4 matrix combining wound depth (Grade 0-3) with infection/ischemia status (Stage A-D).

**Grades (Depth):**
| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | Epithelialized wound (pre-ulcerative) |
| 1 | Superficial wound |
| 2 | Wound penetrates to tendon or capsule |
| 3 | Wound penetrates to bone or joint |

**Stages (Complications):**
| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| A | No infection, no ischemia |
| B | Infection present |
| C | Ischemia present |
| D | Both infection AND ischemia |

**Clinical Pearl:** Stage D wounds have highest amputation risk. Limb salvage strategies must address BOTH perfusion and infection.

**Source:** [WCEI Wagner Scale Blog](https://blog.wcei.net/wagner-scale)

---

### PEDIS Classification

IWGDF research classification using 5 categories:

| Category | Grades | Criteria |
|----------|--------|----------|
| **P**erfusion | 1-3 | 1=no PAD, 2=PAD without CLI, 3=CLI |
| **E**xtent | cm^2 | Measured after debridement |
| **D**epth | 1-3 | 1=superficial, 2=fascia/muscle/tendon, 3=bone/joint |
| **I**nfection | 1-4 | Maps to IDSA mild/moderate/severe |
| **S**ensation | 1-2 | 1=intact, 2=loss of protective sensation |

**Validation:** Demonstrated good diagnostic accuracy for predicting ulcer outcomes. Worse perfusion, larger extent, deeper wound, more severe infection, and loss of sensation were independent predictors of adverse outcomes.

**Source:** [PLOS One - PEDIS Validation Study](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0124739)

---

### SINBAD Classification

Simplified scoring system (0-6 points) recommended by IWGDF for communication:

| Factor | Score 0 | Score 1 |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **S**ite | Forefoot | Midfoot/Hindfoot |
| **I**schemia | Pedal pulses present | Clinical evidence of PAD |
| **N**europathy | Protective sensation intact | Loss of protective sensation |
| **B**acterial infection | None | Present |
| **A**rea | <1 cm^2 | >=1 cm^2 |
| **D**epth | Superficial (skin/subcutaneous) | Deep (muscle/tendon/bone) |

**Interpretation:**
- Score <3: 60% healing at 12 weeks, 0.7% major amputation at 6 months
- Score >=3: 35% healing at 12 weeks, 2.7% major amputation at 6 months

**Source:** [Diabetes Care - SINBAD Study](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/31/5/964/29972/Use-of-the-SINBAD-Classification-System-and-Score)

### WIfI Classification (Society for Vascular Surgery)

Comprehensive staging for chronic limb-threatening ischemia (CLTI):

**Components (each graded 0-3):**

**Wound (W):**
| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No ulcer, no gangrene |
| 1 | Small shallow ulcer, no gangrene |
| 2 | Deep ulcer with exposed bone/joint OR gangrene limited to digits |
| 3 | Extensive deep ulcer OR extensive gangrene |

**Ischemia (I):**
| Grade | ABI | Ankle Pressure | Toe Pressure |
|-------|-----|----------------|--------------|
| 0 | >=0.80 | >100 mmHg | >=60 mmHg |
| 1 | 0.6-0.79 | 70-100 mmHg | 40-59 mmHg |
| 2 | 0.4-0.59 | 50-70 mmHg | 30-39 mmHg |
| 3 | <0.4 | <50 mmHg | <30 mmHg |

**foot Infection (fI):**
| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | No infection |
| 1 | Mild: Local infection, erythema 0.5-2 cm |
| 2 | Moderate: Local infection, erythema >2 cm OR deeper structures |
| 3 | Severe: SIRS, sepsis |

**Clinical Stages and 1-Year Amputation Risk:**
| Stage | Amputation Risk |
|-------|-----------------|
| 1 | 0% |
| 2 | 8% |
| 3 | 11% |
| 4 | 38% |
| 5 | Unsalvageable |

**Source:** [Journal of Vascular Surgery - WIfI System](https://www.jvascsurg.org/article/S0741-5214(13)01515-2/fulltext)

## Infection Severity (IDSA/IWGDF)

### IDSA/IWGDF 2023 Classification

| Grade | Severity | Clinical Findings |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | Uninfected | No purulence or inflammation |
| 2 | Mild | Purulence OR >=2 signs of inflammation (erythema, warmth, tenderness, pain, induration) AND erythema 0.5-2 cm around ulcer AND infection limited to skin/superficial subcutaneous |
| 3 | Moderate | Infection in patient who is systemically well AND metabolically stable BUT has >=1 of: erythema >2 cm, lymphangitis, spread beneath fascia, deep tissue abscess, gangrene, muscle/tendon/joint/bone involvement |
| 4 | Severe | Infection in patient with systemic toxicity OR metabolic instability (fever, chills, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion, vomiting, leukocytosis, severe hyperglycemia, acidosis, azotemia) |

**Key SIRS Criteria for Severe:**
- Temperature >38C or <36C
- Heart rate >90 bpm
- Respiratory rate >20/min or PaCO2 <32 mmHg
- WBC >12,000 or <4,000 or >10% bands

**Source:** [IWGDF/IDSA 2023 Guidelines](https://academic.oup.com/cid/advance-article/doi/10.1093/cid/ciad527/7287196)

## Vascular Assessment

### Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)

| ABI Value | Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------|
| >1.40 | Non-compressible (calcified vessels) - UNRELIABLE |
| 1.00-1.40 | Normal |
| 0.91-0.99 | Borderline |
| 0.71-0.90 | Mild PAD |
| 0.41-0.70 | Moderate PAD |
| <0.40 | Severe PAD (rest pain likely) |
| <0.25 | Critical limb-threatening ischemia |

**CRITICAL:** ABI has poor sensitivity (~35%) in diabetics due to medial arterial calcification. Use TBI instead.

---

### Toe-Brachial Index (TBI)

Preferred in diabetics because digital arteries are typically spared from calcification.

| TBI Value | Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------|
| >=0.75 | PAD unlikely |
| 0.60-0.74 | Borderline |
| <0.60 | PAD present |
| <0.30 | Healing unlikely |

---

### TcPO2 (Transcutaneous Oxygen Pressure)

| TcPO2 Value | Interpretation |
|-------------|----------------|
| >=40 mmHg | Normal, adequate for healing |
| 25-40 mmHg | Compromised, consider revascularization |
| <25 mmHg | Healing unlikely without revascularization |

---

### Urgent Vascular Imaging Indications

Obtain urgent vascular imaging and consider revascularization when:
- Ankle pressure <50 mmHg
- ABI <0.5
- Toe pressure <30 mmHg
- TcPO2 <25 mmHg
- Non-healing wound despite adequate offloading and infection control

**Source:** [IWGDF PAD Guideline 2019](https://iwgdfguidelines.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/04-IWGDF-PAD-guideline-2019.pdf)

## Osteomyelitis Workup

### Clinical Suspicion

**High-Risk Features:**
- Ulcer present >2 weeks
- Ulcer size >2 cm^2
- Positive probe-to-bone test
- Elevated ESR (>70 mm/hr highly suggestive)
- Visible bone or sausage-shaped toe
- Prior history of foot osteomyelitis

---

### Probe-to-Bone (PTB) Test

**Technique:** Insert sterile blunt probe into ulcer base. Positive = hard, gritty sensation of bone.

| Test Performance | Value |
|------------------|-------|
| Sensitivity | 87% (95% CI: 75-93%) |
| Specificity | 83% (95% CI: 65-93%) |
| Positive LR | 4.3-9.4 |

**Interpretation:**
- In HIGH pretest probability setting: Positive PTB highly suggestive of osteomyelitis
- Negative PTB does NOT exclude osteomyelitis
- Best used in combination with imaging

**Source:** [Clinical Infectious Diseases - PTB Systematic Review](https://academic.oup.com/cid/article/63/7/944/2197008)

---

### Imaging Algorithm

**Step 1: Plain Radiographs (X-Ray)**
- Order for ALL suspected DFI
- Sensitivity: 54%, Specificity: 68%
- May take 2-3 weeks for changes to appear
- Look for: cortical erosion, periosteal reaction, sequestrum, involucrum

**Step 2: MRI (Gold Standard)**
- Sensitivity: 90-96%, Specificity: 79-84%
- Indications:
  - Negative or equivocal X-ray with clinical concern
  - Positive PTB
  - Planning surgical debridement/amputation level
  - Non-healing wound despite treatment

**MRI Findings:**
- T1: Low signal in bone marrow (replaces normal fat)
- T2/STIR: High signal in bone marrow
- Post-contrast: Enhancement of bone and surrounding soft tissue

**Caveat:** MRI cannot always distinguish osteomyelitis from Charcot neuroarthropathy

**Step 3: Bone Biopsy (Definitive)**
- Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 99%
- Indications:
  - Definitive diagnosis required
  - Culture/susceptibility needed
  - Non-response to empiric antibiotics

---

### Combined Diagnostic Approach

| Finding Combination | Likelihood |
|--------------------|------------|
| PTB positive + Positive X-ray | Osteomyelitis very likely (>90% probability) |
| PTB positive + Negative X-ray | Order MRI |
| PTB negative + Negative X-ray | Low probability but MRI if clinical concern persists |
| ESR >70 + Ulcer >2 cm | High probability, consider MRI |

**Source:** [PMC - Diagnosing Diabetic Foot Osteomyelitis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4281812/)

## Charcot Foot Recognition

### Definition
Charcot neuroarthropathy (CN) is a progressive destructive condition affecting bones and joints in patients with peripheral neuropathy. It is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY that can lead to irreversible deformity if not promptly addressed.

### Prevalence
Up to 13% of individuals with diabetes

---

### Acute Charcot: Clinical Presentation

**Classic Triad:**
1. Unilateral swollen foot/ankle
2. Warmth (2-8C difference vs. contralateral)
3. Erythema

**Key Features:**
- Usually afebrile with normal vital signs
- Minimal or no pain (due to neuropathy)
- History of minimal or no recalled trauma
- Occurs in patients >40 years with diabetes and peripheral neuropathy

**CRITICAL:** 25% of acute Charcot is MISDIAGNOSED (as cellulitis, gout, DVT, sprain). Average delay to diagnosis: 7 months.

---

### Eichenholtz Classification (Stages)

| Stage | Name | Clinical | Radiographic |
|-------|------|----------|--------------|
| 0 | Pre-clinical | Warm, swollen, painful | Normal X-ray, marrow edema on MRI |
| 1 | Development/Fragmentation | Red, hot, swollen | Osteopenia, fragmentation, subluxation |
| 2 | Coalescence | Decreased warmth/swelling | Absorption of debris, sclerosis |
| 3 | Reconstruction | No inflammation | Remodeling, fusion, stable deformity |

---

### Diagnostic Workup

**Laboratory:**
- CBC, BMP, ESR, CRP (to help distinguish from infection)
- Blood glucose, HbA1c

**Imaging:**
1. **Weight-bearing X-rays:** May be normal early; later shows fragmentation, dislocation
2. **MRI:** Shows bone marrow edema early (before X-ray changes)
3. **Comparison to contralateral foot:** Temperature difference (infrared thermometry if available)

**Differentiating Charcot from Infection:**
| Feature | Charcot | Osteomyelitis |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Open wound | Usually absent | Usually present |
| Fever | Absent | May be present |
| WBC | Normal | Elevated |
| ESR/CRP | Mildly elevated | Significantly elevated |
| MRI pattern | Bilateral marrow edema | Focal bone destruction |

---

### ED Management of Acute Charcot

1. **Immediate offloading:** CAM boot or wheelchair (avoid splint/cast that patient might not follow up to remove)
2. **Strict non-weight-bearing**
3. **Glycemic control**
4. **Urgent referral:** Podiatry or foot/ankle orthopedics within 24-48 hours
5. **Patient education:** Emphasize this is a medical emergency

**Source:** [PMC - Initial Diagnosis and Management for Acute Charcot](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6276967/)

## Antibiotic Selection

### IWGDF/IDSA 2023 Principles

1. Do NOT treat uninfected ulcers with antibiotics (even if osteomyelitis on imaging)
2. Base empiric selection on severity, recent antibiotics, prior resistant organisms, local resistance patterns
3. Obtain cultures BEFORE starting antibiotics when possible
4. Narrow spectrum based on culture results

---

### Empiric Antibiotic Regimens by Severity

#### MILD Infection (Outpatient, Oral)

**Target:** Gram-positive cocci (Staph, Strep)

| Antibiotic | Dose | Duration |
|------------|------|----------|
| Cephalexin | 500 mg QID | 7-14 days |
| Dicloxacillin | 500 mg QID | 7-14 days |
| Amoxicillin-clavulanate | 875/125 mg BID | 7-14 days |
| Clindamycin | 300-450 mg TID | 7-14 days |

**If MRSA suspected (add or substitute):**
| Antibiotic | Dose | Duration |
|------------|------|----------|
| TMP-SMX | 1-2 DS tabs BID | 7-14 days |
| Doxycycline | 100 mg BID | 7-14 days |
| Clindamycin | 300-450 mg TID | 7-14 days |

---

#### MODERATE Infection (May be Outpatient or Inpatient)

**Target:** Gram-positive, gram-negative, +/- anaerobes

**Oral Options:**
| Regimen | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BID | Good broad coverage |
| Levofloxacin 750 mg daily + Clindamycin 450 mg TID | FQ + anaerobic coverage |
| Moxifloxacin 400 mg daily | Monotherapy option |

**IV Options:**
| Regimen | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV q6h | First-line |
| Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q8h | Broader gram-negative |
| Ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV daily + Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h | Alternative |
| Ertapenem 1 g IV daily | Once-daily option |

---

#### SEVERE Infection (Hospitalization Required)

**Target:** Broad-spectrum including MRSA, Pseudomonas (if risk factors), anaerobes

| Regimen | Dose |
|---------|------|
| Vancomycin | 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (target trough 15-20) |
| PLUS one of: | |
| Piperacillin-tazobactam | 4.5 g IV q6h |
| Meropenem | 1 g IV q8h |
| Imipenem-cilastatin | 500 mg IV q6h |
| Cefepime + Metronidazole | 2 g IV q8h + 500 mg IV q8h |

---

### MRSA Risk Factors

- Prior MRSA infection or colonization
- Recent hospitalization or antibiotic use
- Nursing home residence
- Hemodialysis
- HIV infection
- Failed outpatient therapy

**MRSA Nasal PCR:** Negative predictive value >95%. Can de-escalate MRSA coverage if negative.

---

### Duration of Therapy

| Infection Type | Duration |
|----------------|----------|
| Mild soft tissue | 7-14 days |
| Moderate soft tissue | 1-2 weeks (up to 4 weeks if severe PAD or slow healing) |
| Severe soft tissue with debridement | 10 days |
| Osteomyelitis with surgical resection + negative margins | 3 weeks |
| Osteomyelitis with surgical resection + positive margins | 6 weeks |
| Osteomyelitis without surgery | 6 weeks minimum |

**2023 Update:** Reduced post-treatment follow-up for osteomyelitis remission from 12 to 6 months.

**Source:** [IDSA - Diabetic Foot Infections Guidelines](https://www.idsociety.org/practice-guideline/diabetic-foot-infections/)

## Surgical Indications

### Emergency Surgery (Within Hours)

| Indication | Urgency |
|------------|---------|
| Necrotizing fasciitis | IMMEDIATE |
| Gas gangrene | IMMEDIATE |
| Compartment syndrome | IMMEDIATE |
| Severe infection + ischemic limb | IMMEDIATE |
| Wet gangrene with sepsis | IMMEDIATE |

---

### Urgent Surgery (Within 24-48 Hours)

| Indication | Details |
|------------|---------|
| Deep abscess | I&D required |
| Moderate-severe DFI with complications | Per IDSA guidelines |
| Extensive tissue necrosis | Debridement |
| Failed conservative management | Reassess and operate |

---

### Necrotizing Fasciitis Recognition

**Clinical Features:**
- Pain out of proportion to exam (early)
- Rapidly spreading erythema
- Skin necrosis, bullae, crepitus
- Systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, hypotension)
- Elevated WBC, lactate, CRP

**Management:**
1. Aggressive fluid resuscitation
2. Broad-spectrum antibiotics (Vancomycin + Pip-tazo + Clindamycin)
3. Emergent surgical consultation
4. Decompressive fasciotomy +/- debridement
5. ICU admission

**Prognosis:** >75% require amputation due to severity. Prompt treatment improves outcomes.

---

### Amputation Indications

**Major Amputation Criteria:**
- Non-reconstructable vascular disease
- Extensive tissue loss
- Life-threatening sepsis not controlled by debridement
- Functionally useless limb
- Patient preference

**Minor Amputation:** Limited tissue excision at or below ankle. Requires adequate perfusion for healing.

**Level Determination:**
- TcPO2 >25 mmHg at proposed level
- Toe pressure >30 mmHg
- Palpable pulse at level above
- Skin perfusion pressure >=40 mmHg

---

### Dry vs. Wet Gangrene

| Feature | Dry Gangrene | Wet Gangrene |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Appearance | Desiccated, mummified | Edematous, purulent |
| Infection | None | Present |
| Urgency | Can observe for autoamputation | Urgent surgery required |
| Treatment | May demarcate and autoamputate | Surgical amputation |

**Source:** [PMC - Surgical Management of Diabetic Foot Infections](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18512303/)

## Wound Management

### Offloading Devices

**Hierarchy (Most to Least Effective):**

1. **Total Contact Cast (TCC)** - Gold standard
   - Non-removable
   - Redistributes pressure across entire foot
   - Healing rate: ~90% at 12 weeks
   - Requires trained application
   - Cannot use with active infection or severe PAD

2. **Irremovable Cast Walker (iRCW)**
   - Removable walker made irremovable with wrap
   - Equal efficacy to TCC, lower cost
   - Patient cannot remove

3. **Removable Cast Walker (RCW)**
   - Allows wound inspection
   - Healing rate: ~65% at 12 weeks (lower due to non-compliance)
   - Use when frequent wound inspection needed

4. **Half-shoe/Healing sandal**
   - Healing rate: ~58%
   - Less effective but better than nothing

**IWGDF Recommendation:** Non-removable knee-high device (TCC or iRCW) for neuropathic plantar forefoot/midfoot ulcers.

---

### Debridement

**Indications:**
- Remove necrotic tissue
- Reduce bacterial burden
- Stimulate wound healing
- Enable accurate wound assessment

**Types:**
- Sharp/surgical (most effective)
- Enzymatic
- Autolytic
- Mechanical
- Biosurgical (maggot therapy)

**Frequency:** As needed, often weekly in outpatient setting.

---

### Wound Dressings

| Wound Type | Dressing Choice |
|------------|-----------------|
| Dry wound | Hydrogel, hydrocolloid |
| Moist wound | Foam, alginate |
| Heavily exudating | Alginate, foam, hydrofiber |
| Infected wound | Silver-containing, iodine-containing |
| Deep cavity | Alginate rope, hydrofiber |

**IWGDF 2023:** No strong evidence for superiority of any specific advanced dressing. Focus on moisture balance, infection control, and regular assessment.

---

### Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

**Indications:**
- Post-surgical wounds
- Deep cavity wounds
- Wounds with high exudate
- Failed conservative management
- Bridge to surgery

**Contraindications:**
- Active infection (untreated)
- Unexplored fistula
- Malignancy in wound
- Exposed vital structures

**IWGDF 2023:** Recommend against NPWT as adjunctive treatment FOR INFECTION. May use as aid to healing post-operative wounds.

---

### Adjunctive Therapies NOT Recommended (per IWGDF 2023)

- Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF)
- Topical antiseptics
- Silver preparations (for infection treatment)
- Honey
- Bacteriophage therapy
- Hyperbaric oxygen therapy (for infection)
- Topical oxygen therapy

**Source:** [Diabetes Care - Offloading Study](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/24/6/1019/22464/Off-Loading-the-Diabetic-Foot-WoundA-randomized)

## Disposition

### Outpatient Criteria (ALL must be met)

- Mild infection (IDSA Grade 2)
- No systemic toxicity
- No significant comorbidities requiring monitoring
- No deep abscess requiring I&D
- Adequate home support and compliance
- Access to follow-up within 48-72 hours
- No failed prior outpatient treatment

---

### Admission Criteria (ANY warrants hospitalization)

**Infection-Related:**
- Severe infection (IDSA Grade 4)
- Moderate infection with ANY of:
  - Gangrene
  - Deep abscess
  - Necrotizing infection
  - Rapidly progressive cellulitis
  - Critical limb ischemia
  - Compartment syndrome

**Patient Factors:**
- Systemic toxicity or sepsis
- Metabolic instability (DKA, HHS, severe hyperglycemia)
- Need for IV antibiotics (unable to take PO)
- Need for urgent surgery
- Inability to care for self
- Inadequate home support
- Failed outpatient management
- Need for urgent vascular intervention

**Social:**
- Cannot obtain antibiotics
- Cannot reliably offload
- Unreliable follow-up

---

### Surgical Consultation Criteria

**Urgent (<24 hours):**
- Necrotizing fasciitis
- Deep abscess
- Wet gangrene
- Compartment syndrome
- Severe infection

**Semi-Urgent (24-48 hours):**
- Moderate infection with deep involvement
- Osteomyelitis requiring debridement
- Non-healing wound requiring debridement

**Outpatient:**
- Chronic osteomyelitis for elective resection
- Deformity correction

---

### Vascular Surgery Referral

**Urgent:**
- Critical limb ischemia (TBI <0.30, Toe pressure <30, TcPO2 <25)
- Ischemic rest pain
- Non-healing wound with inadequate perfusion

**Semi-Urgent:**
- Moderate PAD with non-healing wound
- Claudication affecting quality of life

---

### Pre-Discharge Checklist

- [ ] Clinically stable
- [ ] Any urgently needed surgery completed
- [ ] Glycemic control established
- [ ] Appropriate antibiotics prescribed (with duration)
- [ ] Offloading device fitted
- [ ] Wound care instructions provided
- [ ] Follow-up arranged (wound clinic, podiatry, vascular)
- [ ] Patient demonstrates understanding of warning signs
- [ ] Access to antibiotics confirmed

**Source:** [The Hospitalist - DFI Management Guidelines](https://www.the-hospitalist.org/hospitalist/article/125956/hospital-medicine-guidelines-management-diabetic-foot-infections/)

## Calculator Concepts

### Calculator 1: Wagner/PEDIS/UT Classification Tool

**Purpose:** Systematically classify diabetic foot ulcers using multiple validated systems.

**Inputs:**
- Wound depth (superficial/tendon/bone)
- Wound size (cm^2)
- Presence of gangrene
- Signs of infection (purulence, erythema extent, systemic symptoms)
- Peripheral pulses present
- Protective sensation intact
- ABI/TBI values (if available)

**Outputs:**
- Wagner Grade (0-5)
- UT Grade + Stage
- PEDIS Score
- SINBAD Score
- Risk stratification

---

### Calculator 2: IDSA Infection Severity Classifier

**Purpose:** Determine infection severity to guide treatment setting and antibiotic selection.

**Inputs:**
- Presence of purulence
- Signs of inflammation (erythema, warmth, tenderness, induration)
- Erythema extent from wound edge
- Depth of infection involvement
- Systemic symptoms (fever, chills, confusion)
- Vital signs (temp, HR, RR, BP)
- Labs (WBC, glucose, pH, lactate)

**Outputs:**
- IDSA Grade (1-4: Uninfected/Mild/Moderate/Severe)
- Recommended treatment setting (Outpatient vs. Admit)
- Empiric antibiotic recommendations
- Duration of therapy

---

### Calculator 3: Vascular Assessment Interpreter

**Purpose:** Interpret ABI, TBI, and TcPO2 values with diabetic-specific considerations.

**Inputs:**
- ABI value (with option for non-compressible)
- TBI value (preferred in diabetics)
- TcPO2 value
- Presence of diabetes
- Presence of ESRD

**Outputs:**
- PAD severity classification
- Wound healing probability
- Need for vascular imaging
- Revascularization recommendation

---

### Calculator 4: Osteomyelitis Probability Calculator

**Purpose:** Estimate probability of osteomyelitis and guide workup.

**Inputs:**
- Ulcer duration (weeks)
- Ulcer size (cm^2)
- Probe-to-bone result
- Visible bone
- ESR value
- CRP value
- X-ray findings
- Prior foot osteomyelitis

**Outputs:**
- Osteomyelitis probability (Low/Moderate/High)
- Recommended imaging
- Bone biopsy indication

---

### Calculator 5: Empiric Antibiotic Selector

**Purpose:** Recommend appropriate empiric antibiotics based on infection severity and risk factors.

**Inputs:**
- IDSA Infection Grade
- MRSA risk factors (prior MRSA, recent hospitalization, nursing home, hemodialysis)
- Pseudomonas risk factors (prior Pseudomonas, water exposure)
- Beta-lactam allergy
- Renal function (CrCl)
- Oral vs IV route preferred
- Pregnancy status

**Outputs:**
- First-line antibiotic regimen with doses
- Alternative regimen for allergies
- Duration recommendation
- De-escalation guidance

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. **25% of acute Charcot is misdiagnosed.** Think of it in any diabetic with a red, hot, swollen foot and normal labs.

2. **ABI is unreliable in diabetics.** Use TBI instead due to medial arterial calcification.

3. **Positive probe-to-bone + positive X-ray = osteomyelitis very likely.** Don't delay treatment for MRI.

4. **Don't treat uninfected ulcers with antibiotics** - even if osteomyelitis is present on imaging. Wait for cultures.

5. **Offloading is as important as antibiotics.** A TCC heals 90% of neuropathic ulcers in 12 weeks.

6. **Wet gangrene is an emergency.** Dry gangrene can wait for demarcation.

7. **Necrotizing fasciitis: early pain is out of proportion, late pain is absent** (due to nerve destruction).

8. **MRSA nasal PCR has >95% negative predictive value.** Use it to de-escalate.

9. **MRI cannot always distinguish osteomyelitis from acute Charcot.** Clinical context matters.

10. **Multidisciplinary care improves outcomes.** ID, vascular, podiatry, wound care, and endocrine.
