## 1. INITIAL ASSESSMENT APPROACH

### Modified ABCs: The "C-ABCDE" Framework (EMRA)

**C - Call the LVAD Coordinator**
- Call immediately while simultaneously performing standard resuscitation
- Most patients/families carry direct access numbers
- Check pump, battery pack, or wallet card for phone number and device info
- Consider early transfer to LVAD center

**A - Auscultation / Airway**
- Listen for the "typical mechanical whirr" of the LVAD device
- Absence of hum = pump not functioning = emergency
- Assess if patient is awake, alert, warm, well-perfused
- Standard airway assessment
- CAUTION: Avoid intubation/positive pressure ventilation if possible (worsens RV failure)

**B - Battery & Blood Pressure**
- Connect LVAD to main power source immediately
- Measure MAP via Doppler (see Physical Exam section)
- Goal MAP: 70-90 mmHg
- Patients are PRELOAD DEPENDENT and AFTERLOAD SENSITIVE

**C - Controller**
- Verify device metrics match baseline
- Concerning alarms involve changes in pulsatility and flow rate
- Record speed, power, flow, PI

**D - Driveline**
- Examine abdominal wall exit site for intact driveline
- Check for signs of infection (erythema, drainage, warmth)
- NEVER twist, bend, cut, or damage the driveline
- Consider X-ray or CT for internal fracture

**E - Echo & EKG**
- Bedside echo comparing to prior baseline
- 12-lead ECG on ALL LVAD patients
- Assess for over- vs. under-distension of ventricles

### Alternative Mnemonic: "DEATHS" for Complications
- **D** - Driveline complications (fracture, infection)
- **D** - Device failure
- **E** - Embolism
- **A** - Arrhythmia
- **T** - Thrombus
- **H** - Hemorrhage
- **S** - Suckdown (suction event)

### Undressing the Patient
- Undress gently; avoid sharp tools that could damage device cables/batteries
- Ensure extra battery is available
- Request trained family member participation

## 2. PHYSICAL EXAM CONSIDERATIONS

### Key Principle: No Pulse Does NOT Mean No Perfusion
- Continuous-flow LVADs produce minimal or zero pulsatility
- Lack of pulse is NORMAL in properly functioning LVAD
- Pulse oximetry is UNRELIABLE without pulsatile flow
- Traditional BP cuffs (oscillometric) are UNRELIABLE

### Blood Pressure Measurement: Doppler Technique (Gold Standard)
1. Place pencil Doppler probe over brachial artery
2. Inflate standard BP cuff proximally on ipsilateral arm
3. Inflate >30 mmHg above last audible flow signal
4. Slowly deflate cuff
5. Record pressure when flow signal FIRST returns = MAP (Return to Flow / RTF)
6. This value approximates MAP, NOT systolic BP

**Without Doppler:** Use stethoscope; first (and likely only) Korotkoff sound = MAP

**Invasive Monitoring:** Low threshold for arterial line placement in critically ill patients
- A-line waveform shows near-flatline with artificial pulse variations
- Most accurate method for continuous monitoring

### MAP Goals
- Target: 70-90 mmHg (most sources)
- Some sources: <80 mmHg to reduce pump workload
- Hypertension threshold: MAP >110 mmHg = adverse event
- Minimum for perfusion: MAP >50 mmHg
- Some patients tolerate lower MAPs with adequate perfusion

### Auscultation
- Normal: "Steady high-pitched motor hum without clunking sounds"
- Listen over precordium or epigastrium (device location varies)
- Absence of hum = pump failure = EMERGENCY
- Clunking/grinding = abnormal, suggests mechanical issue
- Palpate device for abnormal heat (suggests thrombosis)

### Perfusion Assessment (Instead of Pulse)
- Mental status / level of consciousness
- Skin color, temperature, mottling
- Capillary refill time
- Urine output
- Lactate level
- These are "reasonable predictors of adequate flow" (AHA)

### Neurologic Exam
- Screen for focal deficits (stroke risk is high)
- Altered mental status, headache (hemorrhage risk)
- Full neuro exam on every LVAD patient

### Driveline Site Inspection
- Use sterile technique
- Check for erythema, purulent drainage, warmth, tenderness
- Note any exposed cable or device erosion

## 3. DEVICE ASSESSMENT & PARAMETERS

### Components All LVADs Share
- **Inflow cannula**: Draws blood from left ventricle
- **Pump**: Continuous-flow centrifugal or axial device
- **Outflow graft**: Returns blood to ascending aorta
- **Driveline**: Percutaneous cable connecting pump to external controller
- **Controller**: External device displaying parameters and alarms
- **Power source**: Batteries (portable) or base unit (wall power)

### Key Parameters Displayed on Controller

| Parameter | Normal Range | Clinical Meaning |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Speed (RPM)** | Device-specific (see below) | Only manually set parameter; determines pump output |
| **Power (Watts)** | 4-6 W | Work performed by pump; rises with thrombus or high flow |
| **Flow (L/min)** | 4-6 L/min | Estimated cardiac output (calculated from power + speed + Hct) |
| **Pulsatility Index (PI)** | 1-10 | Native LV contractility; higher = more native heart function |

### Device-Specific Normal Parameters

| Device | RPM Range | Flow | Placement |
|--------|-----------|------|-----------|
| **HeartMate 3** | 4,000-6,000 | 4-6 L/min | Intrapericardial |
| **HeartMate II** | 8,800-10,000 | 4-6 L/min | Intra-abdominal |
| **HVAD (HeartWare)** | 2,500-3,500 | 4-6 L/min | Intrapericardial |

### Interpreting Parameter Changes

| Finding | Speed | Power | Flow | PI | Likely Cause |
|---------|-------|-------|------|-----|-------------|
| Hypovolemia/bleeding | -- | Low | Low | Low | Decreased preload |
| Suction event | -- | Low | Low | Low | LV underfilling, cannula against septum |
| Pump thrombosis | -- | HIGH | Falsely HIGH | -- | Clot increasing pump work |
| Complete obstruction | -- | Low | Low | -- | Pump cannot generate flow |
| RV failure | -- | Low | Low | -- | Inadequate LV filling |
| Sepsis/vasodilation | -- | High | High | -- | Decreased afterload |
| Tamponade | -- | Low | Low | Low | External compression |

### Hemodynamic Interpretation with JVP

| VAD Flow | JVP | Likely Diagnosis |
|----------|-----|-----------------|
| Low flow + Low JVP | Low | Bleeding / hypovolemia |
| Low flow + High JVP | High | RV dysfunction, PE, pneumothorax, tamponade |
| High flow | Variable | Vasodilatation (sepsis, vasoactive drugs) |

## 4. ALARM INTERPRETATION

### HeartMate 3 Alarm Classification

**HAZARD ALARMS (Red - Constant Tone) - Life-Threatening, Immediate Action Required**
- **Red heart (flashing)**: Low flow (<2.5 LPM) or pump stoppage - EMERGENT VAD team contact
- **Red battery icon**: <5 minutes power remaining
- **Driveline disconnected**: Pump stops - reconnect immediately
- **Controller fault**: Requires trained professional for controller exchange
- **Electrical fault**: Possible driveline wire fracture - immediate VAD team consultation

**ADVISORY ALARMS (Yellow - Fast Beeping) - Important, Non-Life-Threatening**
- **Yellow battery icon (diamond)**: <15 minutes power remaining - replace battery promptly
- **Yellow triangle**: Various advisory conditions
- **High Watts**: Suggests pump thrombosis (increased energy requirement to spin)

### HVAD (HeartWare) Alarm Classification
- **Solid yellow**: Low priority
- **Flashing yellow**: Medium priority
- **Red**: High priority (e.g., "VAD Stopped," "Critical Battery")
- Reports speed and power only (no PI on controller)
- Waveforms on system monitor estimate LV function

### General Alarm Troubleshooting Steps
1. Check all connections (driveline to controller, controller to power)
2. Ensure batteries are charged; switch to backup if needed
3. Position patient flat before replacing battery
4. Connect to hospital power base unit or patient's own unit
5. If controller fault: use patient's backup controller (patients should carry one)
6. Call VAD coordinator for any alarm you cannot resolve

### Low Battery Warning
- Low battery may trigger automatic speed reduction
- Speed reduction can cause syncope or hemodynamic instability
- Always ensure adequate power supply

## 5. COMMON COMPLICATIONS & MANAGEMENT

### 5A. BLEEDING (Most Common ED Presentation, ~42% frequency)

**Incidence**: Up to 70% of LVAD patients over device lifetime
**GI bleeding**: 15-30% (most common source); 9.7-24.5% with HM3

**Pathophysiology (Multiple Mechanisms)**:
- Acquired von Willebrand disease (high shear forces destroy large VWF multimers)
- Chronic anticoagulation (warfarin + aspirin)
- Lack of pulsatile flow creates GI arteriovenous malformations (AVMs)
- Hepatic congestion
- Hemolytic anemia

**Management**:
- Contact VAD coordinator immediately
- Standard resuscitation, type and screen
- GI: Endoscopy/colonoscopy for source identification
- AVOID reflexive transfusion in hemodynamically stable patients (increases transplant sensitization risk)
- If transfusing: use LEUKOREDUCED, IRRADIATED blood products
- Massive transfusion protocol risks precipitating RV failure
- Anticoagulation reversal: Coordinate with VAD team
  - Vitamin K (primary agent for warfarin reversal)
  - PCC (prothrombin complex concentrate) for urgent reversal
  - Warfarin reversal carries LOW risk for acute pump thrombosis
  - Consider: TXA, desmopressin (DDAVP), FFP, recombinant factor VII
- Octreotide for refractory/recurrent GI bleeding from AVMs:
  - 50-100 mcg SQ BID, or
  - Octreotide LAR 20-30 mg IM monthly
  - Mechanism: Reduces splanchnic flow, enhances platelet aggregation, inhibits angiogenesis

### 5B. PUMP THROMBOSIS (Surgical Emergency)

**Incidence**: 1.4-5% per patient per year (device-dependent); 1.5% at 2 years for HM3
**Average onset**: ~1 year post-implantation
**48% six-month mortality without intervention**

**Presentation**: Poor systemic perfusion, possible pulmonary edema
**Device clues**: High power output, falsely elevated flow readings, abnormal hum, hot device

**Diagnosis**:
- LDH >2.5x upper limit normal (~80% sensitivity, 90% specificity)
- LDH >1,150 IU/L highly suggestive
- Low haptoglobin
- Elevated plasma free hemoglobin
- Hemoglobinuria
- Acute kidney injury
- Echo: Cannula flow velocity >2 m/s, increased aortic valve opening, increasing LV diastolic dimension
- CT with contrast for thrombus

**Management**:
- Immediate heparin and antiplatelet therapy
- Call VAD team / cardiothoracic surgery
- Consider thrombolytics if peri-arrest or surgical exchange unavailable:
  - **tPA Option 1**: 5 mg IV bolus, then 3 mg/hr x 10 hours, then 1 mg/hr up to 48 hours (max 100 mg total)
  - **tPA Option 2**: 10 mg IV bolus, then 1 mg/min x 20 minutes, then 1 mg/hr x 24 hours
  - Outcomes: 70% hemolysis resolution without exchange at 30 days; 95% survival at 30 days; 10% hemorrhagic stroke
- Definitive treatment: Device exchange (surgical)

### 5C. INFECTION (Second Most Common, 30-50% of patients)

**Incidence**: 20% develop LVAD-related infection within first year

**Types**:
- Driveline site infection (most common)
- Pump pocket infection
- Bloodstream infection / sepsis
- Non-LVAD related infections (increased frequency in LVAD population)

**Organisms**: Staphylococcus (most common), Pseudomonas, gram-negative bacteria, fungal pathogens

**Workup**:
- Blood cultures AND driveline site cultures (before antibiotics)
- Abdominal CT for fluid collections around driveline/pump/pocket
- CBC, CRP, procalcitonin

**Management**:
- Cover gram-positives AND gram-negatives empirically
- Standard early sepsis bundle (fluids, early antimicrobials)
- Driveline trauma/traction disrupts skin seal - assess integrity
- Transfer to VAD center for device-specific management
- Long-term suppressive antibiotics often needed

### 5D. RIGHT VENTRICULAR FAILURE (9-40% incidence; 15-20% early post-implant)

**Why it happens**: LVAD unloads LV, increasing venous return to RV. RV may not tolerate increased preload, especially if already dysfunctional.

**Presentation**: Low VAD flows despite mechanical support, volume overload signs (elevated JVP, ascites, edema), pulmonary edema

**Echo findings**: RV:LV ratio >0.6 on apical 4-chamber, IVC <50% collapse with inspiration

**Management**:
- Optimize preload with careful diuresis (avoid aggressive diuresis)
- DO NOT give reflexive fluids unless truly volume-depleted
- Support MAP for RV coronary perfusion (epinephrine, vasopressin, norepinephrine)
- Decrease RV afterload: prevent hypoxia/hypercarbia; inhaled pulmonary vasodilators (iNO, epoprostenol)
- Inotropes for contractility: low-dose epinephrine, dobutamine, milrinone
- AVOID positive pressure ventilation/intubation (worsens RV failure by increasing RV afterload)
- Consider VA-ECMO or RVAD if refractory

### 5E. SUCTION EVENTS ("Suckdown")

**What it is**: Left ventricle collapses from negative pressure when pump tries to pull more blood than available. Inflow cannula adheres to interventricular septum.

**Causes**: Hypovolemia, cannula malposition, RV failure, tamponade, arrhythmia

**Presentation**: Low flow/low power on controller, arrhythmias, hemodynamic instability

**Echo**: Small LV, septal deviation toward LVAD inflow

**Management**:
- IVF bolus (LVADs are preload dependent)
- HM3 automatically decreases speed transiently when detected
- Allow controller to sense low preload and adjust
- Always perform POCUS - treatment varies by etiology
- Contact VAD team for speed adjustment if needed

### 5F. STROKE (6.8% ischemic, 8.4% hemorrhagic; ~10% by 2 years; up to 27% overall)

**Risk factors**: MAP >90, supratherapeutic INR, pump thrombosis

**Ischemic Stroke**:
- Stat non-contrast head CT, CTA head/neck +/- perfusion
- Most patients are CONTRAINDICATED for systemic thrombolytics (on warfarin)
- Endovascular treatment may be option - coordinate with VAD team + neurology
- HM3 is NOT MRI compatible

**Hemorrhagic Stroke**:
- Discontinue or reverse anticoagulation (coordinate with VAD team)
- BP control (MAP target)
- Early neurosurgery + stroke + LVAD consultation
- Median time to resume aspirin: 8 days; warfarin: 14 days

### 5G. ARRHYTHMIAS (Up to 50% incidence)

**Key principle**: VADs are ECG-independent. The pump continues to function during many arrhythmias. ICD discharges are frequent and often inappropriate.

**Impact**: Prolonged ventricular arrhythmias precipitate RV failure through decreased LV preload, low flows, and suction events.

**Management**:
- Treat atrial fibrillation per standard protocols
- Ventricular arrhythmias: volume replacement + pharmacological/electrical cardioversion
  - IV amiodarone (first-line)
  - Lidocaine, procainamide as alternatives
- Cardioversion/defibrillation pad placement: ANTERIOR-POSTERIOR, avoiding driveline and device
- Address reversible causes (electrolytes, ischemia, suction)
- Do NOT aggressively treat asymptomatic arrhythmias (even non-sustained VT)

### 5H. AORTIC REGURGITATION (6-32% at 1 year, 24-33% at 3 years)

**Mechanism**: Decreased aortic valve opening predisposes to leaflet fusion; reverse pressure gradient promotes regurgitation. Creates recirculation loop (LVAD pumps blood into aorta, regurgitates back into LV).

**Presentation**: Low cardiac output despite elevated flows/power, decreased PI. Cardiogenic shock, pulmonary edema, RV failure.

**Management**: Afterload reduction, diuresis if volume-overloaded, possible speed adjustment. Early consultation. May need surgical/transcatheter valve intervention.

### 5I. DRIVELINE COMPLICATIONS

**Fracture**: Assess via chest X-ray; consult cardiac surgery
**Infection**: See infection section above
**Damage**: Never twist, bend, cut the driveline; any damage = immediate VAD team contact

## 6. DEVICE-SPECIFIC INFORMATION

### HeartMate 3 (Abbott) - CURRENTLY DOMINANT DEVICE
- **Type**: Intrapericardial centrifugal-flow with full magnetic levitation
- **Key advantage**: No mechanical bearings = reduced friction = reduced thrombosis (1.5% at 2 years)
- **RPM**: 4,000-6,000
- **Creates artificial pulse**: Periodic speed changes for washout
- **NOT MRI compatible**
- **Controller displays**: Speed, Power, Flow, PI
- **Battery**: Black (3h) or gray (14-17h); NEVER disconnect both simultaneously
- **Target INR**: 2.0-3.0
- **Defibrillation/cardioversion**: Safe, no special precautions (anterior-posterior pads)
- **Superior outcomes vs HVAD**: Less cardiac arrhythmias, less pump thrombosis, less major infection, lower hemorrhagic stroke risk

### HeartMate II (Abbott/Thoratec) - LEGACY DEVICE, STILL ENCOUNTERED
- **Type**: Axial continuous-flow
- **RPM**: 8,800-10,000
- **Placement**: Intra-abdominal (below diaphragm)
- **Target INR**: 1.8-2.5
- **Controller displays**: Speed, Power, Flow, PI
- **Higher thrombosis rates than HM3
- **Defibrillation/cardioversion**: Safe, no special precautions

### HVAD / HeartWare (Medtronic) - DISCONTINUED 2021, ~4,000 PATIENTS STILL IMPLANTED
- **Type**: Centrifugal-flow with magnetic/hydrodynamic levitation
- **RPM**: 2,500-3,500
- **Placement**: Intrapericardial (smaller than HM3)
- **Target INR**: 2.0-3.0
- **Controller displays**: Speed, Power only (no PI on controller)
- **DISCONTINUED June 2021**: Multiple Class I recalls, 91 injuries, 15 deaths
- **Primary safety concern**: Pump restart failures (106 complaints since 2009, 14 deaths from restart issues)
- **Patients advised NOT to have it removed** (surgical risk > device risk)
- **Medtronic provides ongoing support**: 20 specialists globally, centralized engineering team
- **Backup controller with modified software available** for pump restart failures
- **Higher rates of**: Stroke, thromboembolic events, need for subsequent RVAD
- **Defibrillation/cardioversion**: Safe, no special precautions

### HeartMate XVE / HeartMate I - VERY RARE, PULSATILE
- **Type**: Pulsatile flow (volume displacement)
- **HAS a palpable pulse** (unlike continuous-flow devices)
- **Has hand pump** for use during defibrillation/cardioversion
- **Essentially no longer encountered** but may appear in older patients

## 7. CARDIAC ARREST IN VAD PATIENTS

### Critical Paradigm Shift
- **2017 AHA Scientific Statement**: Officially endorsed chest compressions in VAD patients
- Historical fears of cannula dislodgement have been DEBUNKED
- Autopsy studies: No disruption or dislodgement of inflow/outflow cannulas after compressions

### Assessment: "Perfusion Checks" Replace "Pulse Checks"
Traditional pulse checks are meaningless in continuous-flow LVAD patients.

**Instead assess**:
- MAP via Doppler or arterial line
- End-tidal CO2 (most important perfusion indicator in intubated patients)
- Skin color, capillary refill
- Level of consciousness
- Point-of-care echo

### When to Start Chest Compressions (AHA Consensus)
Initiate CPR if:
- Patient is **unresponsive** AND
- **MAP <50 mmHg** (or undetectable) AND/OR
- **ETCO2 <20 mmHg** (in correctly intubated patient) AND/OR
- LVAD is non-functioning (no hum, pump stopped)

**Do NOT rely on**: Absent pulse alone, low SpO2 alone

### CPR Technique
- Standard chest compressions (no modification needed)
- High-quality compressions with minimal interruptions
- Abdominal-only compressions are an alternative approach (limited evidence)

### Defibrillation
- NOT contraindicated in LVAD patients
- **Pad placement**: ANTERIOR-POSTERIOR preferred
- Do NOT place pads directly over the LVAD device or driveline
- Never disconnect LVAD components before cardioversion/defibrillation
- All ACLS medications permitted: epinephrine, amiodarone, lidocaine, etc.

### Cardiac Arrest Algorithm
1. Assess responsiveness and perfusion (NOT pulse)
2. Verify LVAD function: Listen for hum, check controller
3. Troubleshoot device: Check power, connections, batteries
4. If LVAD non-functioning AND no perfusion: Begin CPR
5. If LVAD functioning but MAP <50 AND ETCO2 <20: Begin CPR
6. Attach defibrillator (anterior-posterior pads)
7. Standard ACLS: Epinephrine, amiodarone, defibrillation per rhythm
8. Bedside echo: Assess LV/RV size, contractility, tamponade
9. Address reversible causes (hypovolemia, electrolytes, ischemia, PE, tamponade)
10. Contact VAD team emergently
11. Consider VA-ECMO for bridge-to-transplant patients (<60 minutes per JBS 2024)
12. Post-arrest: Standard care including targeted temperature management and early PCI when indicated

### Special Populations
- Patients with ICD: ICD may fire appropriately or inappropriately; does not change management
- Patients with hand pump (HM XVE): Use hand pump during arrest

## 8. ANTICOAGULATION MANAGEMENT

### Standard Regimen
All continuous-flow LVAD patients are on dual antithrombotic therapy:
- **Warfarin** (vitamin K antagonist) - primary anticoagulant
- **Aspirin 81-325 mg daily** - antiplatelet

### INR Targets by Device
| Device | Target INR |
|--------|-----------|
| HeartMate 3 | 2.0-3.0 |
| HeartMate II | 1.8-2.5 |
| HVAD | 2.0-3.0 |

### Time in Therapeutic Range (TTR)
- Meta-analysis: TTR only 46.6% in CF-LVAD patients despite intense monitoring
- Both sub- and supra-therapeutic INR increase risk of complications

### Bleeding Management & Anticoagulation Reversal
- **Assess severity** as with any anticoagulated patient
- **Vitamin K**: Primary reversal agent for warfarin
- **PCC (Prothrombin Complex Concentrate)**: For urgent/life-threatening reversal
- **FFP**: Alternative but less effective; thrombotic risk concerns
- **Warfarin reversal carries LOW risk for acute pump thrombosis** - do not withhold in life-threatening bleeding
- Coordinate with VAD team on timing of resumption
- Hemorrhagic stroke: Median resume aspirin 8 days, warfarin 14 days

### DOACs (Direct Oral Anticoagulants)
- NOT standard of care for LVADs
- Dabigatran was inferior to warfarin for mechanical heart valves (RELIED study)
- Apixaban and rivaroxaban: May be considered in "warfarin failure" only
- Some emerging data on apixaban with HM3 specifically

### Coagulation Paradox
LVAD patients are simultaneously at HIGH THROMBOTIC and HIGH BLEEDING risk:
- Thrombotic: Foreign body, reduced EF, stasis
- Bleeding: Anticoagulation + antiplatelet + acquired VWD + hepatic congestion + loss of pulsatile flow

## 9. IMAGING CONSIDERATIONS

### MRI: CONTRAINDICATED
- All current LVADs contain ferromagnetic materials
- NO MRI-compatible LVAD exists
- For stroke: Use CT/CTA instead

### Echocardiography: PRIMARY MODALITY
- **Bedside/POCUS**: First-line for acute assessment
- Assess:
  - LV and RV size (compare to baseline)
  - Inflow cannula position
  - Inflow/outflow velocities (>2 m/s suggests obstruction)
  - Valvular regurgitation (especially aortic)
  - IVC for volume status
  - Pericardial effusion / tamponade
  - RV:LV ratio (>0.6 suggests RV failure)

**Echo-Based Differential**:
| Echo Finding | Diagnosis |
|-------------|-----------|
| Small RV | Inadequate preload / hypovolemia |
| Large RV + Small LV | PE, RV STEMI, pulmonary hypertension |
| Large RV + Large LV | Pump thrombosis or obstruction |
| Small LV, septal shift | Suction event |

### CT Scan
- Preferred for evaluating outflow graft complications (kinking, obstruction)
- Better cannula visualization than echo (no acoustic shadowing)
- Head CT: Stat for any neurologic change (stroke workup)
- Abdominal CT: Driveline infection with fluid collections
- CTA: Outflow graft, PE evaluation

### Chest X-Ray
- Identifies device type and ICD presence
- Baseline VAD positioning / cannula position
- Driveline fracture assessment
- Pneumothorax, pulmonary edema
- Supplementary role (not primary diagnostic tool)

## 10. MEDICATIONS: WHAT TO USE & AVOID

### Safe to Use
- All standard ACLS medications (epinephrine, amiodarone, lidocaine, atropine)
- IV fluids for hypovolemia (but cautiously - risk of RV overload)
- Heparin (for thrombosis)
- Antibiotics (standard sepsis protocols)
- Magnesium, calcium supplementation
- ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers (for chronic HTN management)
- Hydralazine (oral, for ED hypertension management)

### Use with Caution
- **Vasopressors**: Increase afterload, which the LVAD pumps against
  - Norepinephrine: First-line vasopressor if needed
  - Low-dose epinephrine: Improves contractility with minimal metabolic effects
  - Vasopressin: May be used for RV perfusion support
- **Inotropes**:
  - Dobutamine: Preferred in emergency settings (shorter half-life)
  - Milrinone: Preferred for chronic/subacute RV failure
  - Low-dose epinephrine: Improves contractility
- **IV fluid boluses**: Risk precipitating RV failure; use judiciously
- **Blood transfusion**: Increases sensitization for transplant candidates; use leukoreduced/irradiated

### Avoid or Use Extreme Caution
- **IV push vasopressors/inotropes**: Immediate bolus pushes afterload up suddenly; LVAD cannot pump against this, causing acute pulmonary edema and RV failure spiral
- **Aggressive diuresis** without clear indication (may worsen preload)
- **Positive pressure ventilation / intubation** when avoidable (increases RV afterload, worsens RV failure)
- **DOACs for anticoagulation** (not standard; dabigatran inferior to warfarin for mechanical devices)
- **Systemic thrombolytics for ischemic stroke** (most patients on warfarin = contraindication)

## 11. WHEN TO CALL THE VAD COORDINATOR

### ALWAYS - Call on Every LVAD ED Visit
The universal recommendation across all guidelines: **Contact the VAD coordinator / nearest LVAD center immediately for ANY LVAD patient presenting to the ED.**

### Emergent Transfer to VAD Center Required For:
- Cardiac tamponade
- Mechanical VAD failure (pump stopped, controller fault)
- Pump thrombosis
- Neurologic events (stroke)
- Need for emergency noncardiac surgery
- Driveline damage or electrical fault
- Arrhythmias causing hemodynamic instability
- Suction events not responsive to fluids
- Aortic regurgitation with hemodynamic compromise
- Any situation where device parameters cannot be interpreted

### Information to Have Ready for VAD Team:
- Device type and serial number
- Current controller readings (speed, power, flow, PI)
- Active alarms
- Patient's baseline parameters (patient/family may know)
- Current INR
- Hemolysis labs (LDH, haptoglobin)
- Clinical status and vital signs

## 12. LAB WORKUP

### Standard Labs for All LVAD ED Presentations
- **CBC**: Anemia assessment, platelet count
- **CMP**: Electrolytes, renal function, liver function
- **Coagulation**: PT/INR/PTT (critical for anticoagulation management)
- **Troponin**: Myocardial injury
- **BNP/NT-proBNP**: Device malfunction, RV failure assessment
- **Lactate**: Perfusion adequacy
- **ABG**: If critically ill (difficult to obtain from pulse ox)
- **Type and screen**: Anticipate bleeding

### Hemolysis Panel (If Pump Thrombosis Suspected)
- **LDH**: >2.5x ULN suggests thrombosis; >1,150 IU/L highly suggestive
- **Haptoglobin**: Low/undetectable
- **Plasma free hemoglobin**: Elevated
- **Indirect bilirubin**: Elevated
- **Urinalysis**: Hemoglobinuria
- **Fibrinogen**: May be consumed
- **Consider TEG** (thromboelastography)

### Additional Labs as Indicated
- Blood cultures (if infection suspected - before antibiotics)
- Driveline site cultures
- CRP, procalcitonin (infection markers)
- CK-MB, myoglobin (cardiac injury)

## 13. DISPOSITION

### General Rule
"Almost ALL LVAD patients presenting to the ED will require admission or transfer to a site of expertise."

### Admission Criteria (Nearly Universal)
- Any device alarm
- Any hemodynamic instability
- Any suspected complication (bleeding, infection, thrombosis, stroke)
- Altered mental status
- Abnormal device parameters

### Transfer to VAD Center
- Cardiac tamponade
- Mechanical device failure
- Pump thrombosis (surgical emergency)
- Stroke (both ischemic and hemorrhagic)
- Emergency surgery needed
- Driveline damage / electrical fault
- Suction events not responding to fluids
- Aortic regurgitation with hemodynamic compromise

### Hub-Spoke Model
- Local hospitals: Initial stabilization
- VAD center: Definitive care for device-specific emergencies
- Shared-care model improves outcomes

## CLINICAL PEARLS SUMMARY (For Quick Reference)

1. **No pulse is NORMAL** in continuous-flow LVAD patients
2. **Always call VAD coordinator** - for every ED visit
3. **Listen for the hum** - absence = pump failure = emergency
4. **MAP via Doppler** is the gold standard for BP measurement (goal 70-90)
5. **Preload dependent, afterload sensitive** - the fundamental LVAD hemodynamic principle
6. **Chest compressions ARE safe** - AHA endorsed 2017; no proven cannula dislodgement
7. **CPR criteria**: Unresponsive + MAP <50 + ETCO2 <20
8. **Defibrillator pads**: Anterior-posterior, NOT over device
9. **Pump thrombosis**: High power + hemolysis labs = surgical emergency
10. **GI bleeding** is the #1 ED presentation; acquired vWD is the mechanism
11. **Avoid intubation** if possible (worsens RV failure)
12. **No IV push vasopressors** - causes acute afterload spike and RV failure spiral
13. **MRI is CONTRAINDICATED** in all LVAD patients
14. **Blood products**: Leukoreduced and irradiated (protects transplant candidacy)
15. **HVAD discontinued 2021** but ~4,000 patients still have them; higher complication rates
16. **Nearly all LVAD ED patients require admission or transfer to VAD center**
17. **Bedside echo** is essential: LV/RV size tells the story
18. **LDH >2.5x ULN** = think pump thrombosis
19. **Warfarin reversal is LOW risk** for acute thrombosis in life-threatening bleeding
20. **The patient and family are your best resource** - they know their baseline parameters
