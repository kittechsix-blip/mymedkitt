## Overview and ED Approach

Joint pain is a common pediatric presentation accounting for approximately 5% of all pediatric ED encounters. While most cases are benign and self-limiting, the differential includes life- and limb-threatening conditions that require urgent recognition.

### The "Big 6" Not to Miss
1. **Septic arthritis** - Medical emergency, hours to irreversible joint damage
2. **Osteomyelitis** - Can present as joint pain, especially near joints
3. **Malignancy** - Leukemia presents with bone/joint pain in 25-43% of cases
4. **SCFE** - Orthopedic emergency if unstable
5. **Non-accidental injury** - Always consider in young children
6. **Inflammatory arthritis** - Requires timely diagnosis to prevent complications

### Initial Assessment Framework
1. **Is the child sick or well?** - Septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, and malignancy can present with systemically ill children
2. **Is there true arthritis?** - Look for joint swelling, warmth, effusion, decreased ROM
3. **Is it monoarticular or polyarticular?**
4. **What is the age?** - Guides differential diagnosis significantly
5. **Is there fever?** - Key discriminating factor

## Transient Synovitis vs Septic Arthritis

This is the classic pediatric hip dilemma. Both present with atraumatic, acutely irritable hip with limp, possible fever, refusal to bear weight, limited ROM, and/or joint effusion.

### Transient Synovitis (Toxic Synovitis)
- **Most common cause** of hip pain in children under 10 years
- **Age range:** 3-8 years (peak 4-5 years)
- **Etiology:** Post-viral inflammation (URTI in ~1/3 of cases)
- **Presentation:** Child appears well, usually afebrile or low-grade fever, mild limp
- **Duration:** Self-limiting, resolves in 1-2 weeks
- **Lab findings:** Normal or mildly elevated inflammatory markers

### Septic Arthritis
- **Medical emergency** - Cartilage destruction begins within 8 hours
- **Presentation:** Child appears ill, high fever, refuses to bear weight, severe pain
- **Position of comfort:** Hip held in flexion, abduction, external rotation
- **Lab findings:** Significantly elevated WBC, ESR, CRP

### Key Differentiating Features

| Feature | Transient Synovitis | Septic Arthritis |
|---------|---------------------|------------------|
| Appearance | Well | Ill/toxic |
| Fever | Absent or low-grade (<38.5C) | High (>38.5C) |
| Weight bearing | Usually preserved | Refuses |
| ESR | <20 mm/hr | >40 mm/hr |
| CRP | <20 mg/L | >20 mg/L |
| WBC | <12,000/mm3 | >12,000/mm3 |

**Critical Point:** Presence of hip effusion on ultrasound CANNOT distinguish between transient synovitis and septic arthritis. Effusion is present in 46-86% of transient synovitis cases.

## Kocher and Caird Criteria

### Original Kocher Criteria (1999)
Four clinical predictors to differentiate septic hip from transient synovitis:

1. **History of fever** (>38.5C)
2. **Non-weight bearing** on affected side
3. **ESR >40 mm/hr**
4. **WBC >12,000/mm3**

#### Probability of Septic Arthritis by Kocher Criteria
| # Criteria | Probability |
|------------|-------------|
| 0 | <0.2% |
| 1 | 3.0% |
| 2 | 40.0% |
| 3 | 93.1% |
| 4 | 99.6% |

### Caird Modification (2006)
Added **CRP >20 mg/L** as fifth criterion

#### Probability by Modified Kocher-Caird (5 criteria)
| # Criteria | Probability |
|------------|-------------|
| 0 | 16.9% |
| 1 | 36.7% |
| 2 | 62.4% |
| 3 | 82.6% |
| 4 | 93.1% |
| 5 | 97.5% |

### Important Limitations (2024 Evidence Update)

**The Kingella kingae Problem:**
- Kocher/Caird criteria were developed before K. kingae was recognized as major pathogen
- K. kingae infections often present with milder inflammatory response
- May explain culture-negative cases and false-negative predictions

**Validation Concerns:**
- External validation studies show lower predictive values than original
- Sultan and Hughes (2010): Only 59.9% probability when all 5 criteria positive
- When ALL criteria are ABSENT: essentially rules out septic arthritis

**Best Independent Predictors:**
- **CRP >20 mg/L** - strongest single predictor
- **Refusal to bear weight** - OR 14.5
- Combined: If both CRP <20 AND weight-bearing, <1% chance of septic arthritis

### Clinical Application
- Use criteria to STRATIFY risk, not to definitively diagnose
- Low risk (0 criteria): Can observe with close follow-up
- Intermediate risk: Strongly consider arthrocentesis
- High risk: Arthrocentesis mandatory, prepare for OR
- **When in doubt, tap the joint**

## Septic Arthritis in Children

### Epidemiology
- Incidence: 4-10 per 100,000 children annually
- Male:female ratio 2:1
- Most common joint: Knee (40%), Hip (25%), Ankle (15%)
- Peak age: <3 years

### Age-Based Pathogens

#### Neonates (<3 months)
| Pathogen | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Group B Streptococcus | Most common in neonates |
| Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA/MRSA) | Always cover |
| Gram-negative bacilli | E. coli, Klebsiella |
| Neisseria gonorrhoeae | Consider maternal transmission |

#### Infants/Young Children (3 months - 5 years)
| Pathogen | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Kingella kingae** | Most common 6mo-4yr, often culture-negative |
| Staphylococcus aureus | Still most common overall |
| Streptococcus pneumoniae | Decreased with PCV vaccination |
| Group A Streptococcus | Consider if pharyngitis history |

#### Older Children/Adolescents (>5 years)
| Pathogen | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Staphylococcus aureus | Most common at any age |
| Streptococcus pyogenes | Group A strep |
| Neisseria gonorrhoeae | Sexually active adolescents |

### Kingella kingae: The Emerging Pathogen
- **Age:** 6-36 months (peak 12-24 months)
- **Presentation:** Often less toxic-appearing, lower inflammatory markers
- **Diagnosis:** Requires PCR (often culture-negative)
- **Source:** Colonizes oropharynx, often follows viral URI
- **Clue:** "Culture-negative septic arthritis" in toddler = assume Kingella

### Empiric Antibiotic Therapy

#### Based on MRSA Prevalence

**Low MRSA Prevalence (<10-15%)**
| Age | First-Line | Alternative |
|-----|------------|-------------|
| <3 months | Cefazolin + Gentamicin OR Nafcillin + Cefotaxime | Vancomycin + Cefotaxime |
| 3mo-5yr | Cefazolin | Clindamycin |
| >5yr | Cefazolin | Clindamycin |

**High MRSA Prevalence (>10-15%)**
| Age | First-Line | Notes |
|-----|------------|-------|
| All ages | Vancomycin or Clindamycin | Clindamycin only if local resistance <10% |

#### Pediatric Antibiotic Dosing

| Antibiotic | Dose | Frequency | Max Dose |
|------------|------|-----------|----------|
| **Cefazolin** | 40 mg/kg/dose | IV q8h | 2000 mg/dose |
| **Clindamycin** | 10-13 mg/kg/dose | IV q6-8h | 900 mg/dose |
| **Vancomycin** | 15 mg/kg/dose | IV q6h | Requires TDM |
| **Nafcillin/Oxacillin** | 50 mg/kg/dose | IV q6h | 2000 mg/dose |
| **Cefotaxime** | 50 mg/kg/dose | IV q8h | 2000 mg/dose |

### Synovial Fluid Analysis

| Parameter | Septic | Inflammatory | Non-inflammatory |
|-----------|--------|--------------|------------------|
| WBC count | >50,000/uL | 2,000-50,000/uL | <2,000/uL |
| PMN % | >90% | 50-80% | <25% |
| Glucose | Decreased | Normal-low | Normal |
| Gram stain | May be positive | Negative | Negative |

**Pearl:** Synovial WBC >100,000/uL is highly specific for septic arthritis

## Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)

JIA is the most common chronic rheumatic disease in children, defined as arthritis of unknown etiology beginning before age 16 and persisting for at least 6 weeks.

### ILAR Classification (7 Subtypes)

#### 1. Oligoarticular JIA (50-80% of cases)
- **Definition:** ≤4 joints in first 6 months
- **Subtypes:** Persistent (stays ≤4) vs Extended (>4 after 6 months)
- **Demographics:** Peak 2-4 years, female predominance
- **Pattern:** Asymmetric, large joints (knee most common)
- **ANA positive:** Up to 70%
- **Key complication:** Chronic anterior uveitis (20-30% of ANA+ patients)

#### 2. Polyarticular JIA, RF-negative (15-25%)
- **Definition:** ≥5 joints, RF negative
- **Demographics:** Bimodal (2-4 years, 10-14 years), female predominance
- **Pattern:** Symmetric, large and small joints

#### 3. Polyarticular JIA, RF-positive (5%)
- **Definition:** ≥5 joints, RF positive
- **Demographics:** Adolescent girls
- **Pattern:** Similar to adult RA, aggressive erosive disease
- **Prognosis:** Often persistent into adulthood

#### 4. Systemic JIA (5-15%)
- **Requires:** Arthritis + quotidian fever (≥2 weeks) + ≥1 of:
  - Salmon-colored evanescent rash
  - Generalized lymphadenopathy
  - Hepatosplenomegaly
  - Serositis
- **Labs:** Very high inflammatory markers, anemia, thrombocytosis
- **Complications:** Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS)

#### 5. Enthesitis-Related Arthritis (ERA)
- **Demographics:** Boys >8 years
- **Pattern:** Lower extremity predominant, may develop axial involvement
- **HLA-B27:** Positive in many
- **Associated:** Inflammatory bowel disease, acute anterior uveitis

#### 6. Psoriatic Arthritis
- **Requires:** Arthritis + psoriasis OR arthritis + 2 of:
  - Dactylitis
  - Nail pitting/onycholysis
  - First-degree relative with psoriasis
- **Pattern:** Can be oligoarticular or polyarticular

#### 7. Undifferentiated Arthritis
- Does not fit other categories or fits >1 category

### When to Suspect JIA in the ED
- **Chronic:** Symptoms >6 weeks (acute = more concerning for infection)
- **Morning stiffness** that improves with activity
- **Swelling > pain** (opposite of septic arthritis)
- **No fever** (except systemic JIA)
- **Normal or mildly elevated** inflammatory markers (except systemic JIA)
- **Not acutely toxic-appearing**

### ED Disposition for Suspected JIA
- If stable and infection ruled out: Outpatient referral to pediatric rheumatology
- Start NSAIDs if appropriate
- Urgent ophthalmology referral if uveitis suspected
- Do NOT start steroids without rheumatology input

## Reactive Arthritis

### Post-Viral Reactive Arthritis
- Most common infectious etiology in children
- Occurs concurrent with or 2-4 weeks after viral infection
- Common viruses: Rubella, mumps, hepatitis B, parvovirus B19, EBV
- Self-limiting, resolves in ~6 weeks
- Treatment: NSAIDs, supportive care

### Post-Enteric Reactive Arthritis
- Classic pathogens: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia
- Incidence: 5-9 per 100,000 children
- Onset: 1-4 weeks after GI illness
- Pattern: Oligoarthritis, lower extremity predominant
- HLA-B27 association: Yes
- May become chronic

### Post-Streptococcal Reactive Arthritis (PSRA)
See next section for detailed comparison with ARF

## Post-Streptococcal Reactive Arthritis vs Acute Rheumatic Fever

This distinction is critical because management differs significantly.

### Key Differentiating Features

| Feature | PSRA | ARF |
|---------|------|-----|
| Latent period | <10 days | 2-3 weeks |
| Arthritis pattern | Non-migratory, can be prolonged | Migratory, short-lived |
| Arthritis duration | Weeks to months | Days (rarely >3 weeks) |
| Response to NSAIDs | Slow/incomplete | Dramatic, rapid |
| Age distribution | Bimodal (8-14, 21-37) | Peak ~12 years |
| Carditis | Very rare, can occur late | Major criterion, early |
| HLA-B27 | Normal population frequency | Not associated |

### 2015 Revised Jones Criteria for ARF

**Diagnosis requires:** Evidence of preceding GAS infection + 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor criteria

#### Major Criteria
| Low-Risk Populations | Moderate/High-Risk Populations |
|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| Carditis (clinical or subclinical) | Carditis (clinical or subclinical) |
| Polyarthritis | Monoarthritis, polyarthritis, or polyarthralgia |
| Chorea | Chorea |
| Erythema marginatum | Erythema marginatum |
| Subcutaneous nodules | Subcutaneous nodules |

#### Minor Criteria
| Low-Risk | Moderate/High-Risk |
|----------|-------------------|
| Fever ≥38.5C | Fever ≥38.0C |
| ESR ≥60 mm/hr and/or CRP ≥3.0 mg/dL | ESR ≥30 mm/hr and/or CRP ≥3.0 mg/dL |
| Prolonged PR interval | Prolonged PR interval |

**Note:** Monoarthritis is now a major criterion in moderate/high-risk populations only

### Management of PSRA
1. **Eradicate GAS:** Treat as if active infection
2. **Secondary prophylaxis:** Recommended for 1 year with normal initial echo
3. **Echocardiography:** Baseline and repeat in 1-2 weeks
4. **NSAIDs:** For symptom relief
5. **Monitor:** For delayed carditis (rare but described)

## Lyme Arthritis

### Epidemiology
- Most common manifestation of late Lyme disease in children
- Children twice as likely as adults to develop arthritis
- Endemic areas: Northeast, upper Midwest US (CT, DE, MD, MA, MN, NJ, NY, PA, RI, WI)

### Clinical Features
- **Classic presentation:** Large, swollen knee with surprisingly little pain
- **Timing:** Weeks to months after tick bite (often no recalled bite)
- **Pattern:** Monoarticular or pauciarticular, large joints
- **Unique:** Significant swelling with retained activity/mobility
- **Recurrent:** May wax and wane without treatment

### Diagnostic Approach
1. **Clinical suspicion** in endemic areas with unexplained knee effusion
2. **Two-tier serologic testing:**
   - First: ELISA or immunofluorescence assay
   - If positive/equivocal: Western blot confirmation
3. **Rapid testing:** Sofia IgG (sensitivity 100%, specificity 96.4%)

### Differentiating from Septic Arthritis
| Feature | Lyme Arthritis | Septic Arthritis |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Pain | Minimal/mild | Severe |
| Systemic symptoms | Absent | Present |
| Kocher criteria | ≤1 | Multiple |
| CRP | Elevated | Very elevated |
| Weight bearing | Often preserved | Refuses |

### Treatment

#### Children <8 years
| Antibiotic | Dose | Duration |
|------------|------|----------|
| **Amoxicillin** | 50 mg/kg/day divided TID | 28 days |
| Alternative: Azithromycin | 10 mg/kg day 1, then 5 mg/kg days 2-10 | 10 days |

#### Children ≥8 years
| Antibiotic | Dose | Duration |
|------------|------|----------|
| **Doxycycline** | 4.4 mg/kg/day divided BID (max 200mg/day) | 28 days |
| Alternative: Amoxicillin | 50 mg/kg/day divided TID | 28 days |

**Note:** Short-course doxycycline (≤21 days) now considered safe in all pediatric ages

### If Treatment Failure
- If minimal/no response to oral antibiotics: IV ceftriaxone 2-4 weeks
- Consider NSAID trial for antibiotic-refractory Lyme arthritis

## Osteomyelitis Presenting as Joint Pain

### The Diagnostic Challenge
- Osteomyelitis near joints can mimic septic arthritis
- Initial ED diagnosis only correct 42% of the time
- Children may appear well and be afebrile
- Normal WBC does not exclude diagnosis

### Clinical Presentation
- Bone pain, often near joint
- Limp or refusal to use limb
- Point tenderness over bone (not just joint)
- May have low-grade or no fever
- Pseudo-paralysis in infants

### Red Flags Suggesting Osteomyelitis over Transient Synovitis
- Inability to bear weight
- Elevated CRP
- Elevated ESR
- Point tenderness over metaphysis

### Imaging
- **X-ray:** May be normal for 1-2 weeks
- **MRI:** Most sensitive and specific; shows bone marrow edema early
- **Ultrasound:** Can show subperiosteal abscess

### Relationship to Septic Arthritis
- Can occur together (especially hip in infants)
- Transphyseal vessels in infants allow spread from metaphysis to epiphysis/joint
- Always consider both when evaluating pediatric MSK infections

## Malignancy Red Flags

### Leukemia and Bone/Joint Pain
- **Prevalence:** Bone/joint pain in 25-43% of ALL presentations
- **Mechanism:** Leukemic infiltration of bone marrow, periosteum
- **Challenge:** Often misdiagnosed as JIA, growing pains, or trauma

### Red Flag Signs (CRITICAL)

#### Pain Characteristics
- Severe pain out of proportion to exam findings
- Night pain that wakes child from sleep
- Pain localized to bone (not joint)
- Progressive, unremitting pain
- Pain limiting normal daily activities

#### Systemic Features
- Unexplained fever
- Fatigue/lethargy
- Weight loss
- Pallor
- Easy bruising/petechiae
- Lymphadenopathy
- Hepatosplenomegaly

#### Laboratory Red Flags
- Thrombocytopenia (<300 x 10^9/L)
- Anemia (unexplained)
- Neutropenia (<2 x 10^9/L)
- Elevated LDH
- Elevated uric acid

### Key Distinguishing Features: Leukemia vs JIA

| Feature | Leukemia | JIA |
|---------|----------|-----|
| Pain:swelling ratio | Pain >> swelling | Swelling >> pain |
| Morning stiffness | Absent | Present |
| Hepatosplenomegaly/LAD | Present | Absent (except systemic JIA) |
| Platelet count | Low/normal | Normal/elevated |
| Anemia | Common | Uncommon |
| Night pain | Prominent | Uncommon |

### When to Get Bone Marrow
Consider bone marrow examination if child with >1 month musculoskeletal pain has:
1. Hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, or lymphadenopathy, OR
2. Fever or elevated inflammatory markers + non-articular bone pain + systemic symptoms (fatigue, weight loss) + neutrophils <2 x 10^9/L or platelets <300 x 10^9/L

## Henoch-Schonlein Purpura (HSP) Arthritis

### Overview
- Most common vasculitis in children
- IgA-mediated small vessel vasculitis
- Peak age: 3-15 years
- Male > female

### Classic Tetrad
1. **Palpable purpura** (100%) - typically lower extremities/buttocks
2. **Arthritis/arthralgia** (50-80%)
3. **Abdominal pain** (50-75%)
4. **Renal involvement** (20-50%)

### Arthritis Characteristics
- **Joints affected:** Large joints (knees, ankles most common); elbows, wrists
- **Pattern:** Oligoarticular, periarticular swelling
- **Quality:** Painful but often lacks warmth, erythema, effusion
- **Key feature:** Non-erosive, no permanent deformity
- **Duration:** Resolves in days to weeks
- **May precede rash:** 25% of cases

### Treatment of HSP Arthritis
| Severity | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|
| Mild | Supportive care, rest |
| Moderate | NSAIDs (avoid if renal involvement) |
| Moderate (renal concern) | Acetaminophen |
| Severe | Prednisone 1-2 mg/kg/day x 1-2 weeks |

**Important:** Steroids relieve symptoms but do NOT prevent renal disease

### Monitoring
- Urinalysis weekly x 4 weeks, then monthly x 6 months
- Blood pressure monitoring
- Nephrology referral if hematuria, proteinuria, or hypertension

## Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)

### Overview
- Most common hip disorder in adolescents
- Femoral head slips posteriorly and inferiorly off femoral neck
- **Orthopedic emergency if unstable**

### Demographics
- **Age:** 8-15 years (girls ~12, boys ~13)
- **Risk factors:** Obesity, male sex, African American
- **Bilateral:** 30% will slip contralateral hip (70% within 18 months)

### Classification
| Type | Definition | Ability to Walk | Urgency |
|------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Stable** | Gradual onset | Can bear weight | Urgent surgery |
| **Unstable** | Acute, severe | Cannot bear weight | Emergency surgery |

### Clinical Presentation
- Hip, groin, thigh, or KNEE pain (referred pain in 1/3)
- Limp (antalgic gait)
- Externally rotated leg
- Obligate external rotation with hip flexion (pathognomonic)
- Decreased internal rotation

### **CRITICAL: Commonly Missed Diagnosis**
- Knee pain without hip complaints: ALWAYS examine the hip
- "Growing pains" in obese adolescent: suspect SCFE
- Any adolescent with limp or hip stiffness

### Diagnosis
- **AP and frog-leg lateral X-rays** (stable SCFE)
- **AP and cross-table lateral** (unstable SCFE - do not frog-leg!)
- **Klein's line:** Line along superior femoral neck should intersect epiphysis; if not = positive Trethowan sign
- **MRI:** If X-ray normal but clinical suspicion high

### Management
- **Non-weight bearing immediately**
- **Urgent orthopedic consultation**
- **Surgical treatment:** In-situ screw fixation
- **Complications of delay:** Avascular necrosis (AVN), chondrolysis

## Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease

### Overview
- Idiopathic avascular necrosis of femoral head
- Self-limiting but may cause permanent deformity
- Age: 4-8 years (range 2-15)
- Male:female 5:1
- Bilateral: 10%

### Clinical Presentation
- Insidious onset limp (abductor lurch)
- Hip, groin, thigh, or knee pain (referred)
- Intermittent symptoms initially
- Pain worse with activity, better with rest
- Limited hip ROM (especially internal rotation and abduction)

### Imaging
| Modality | Findings |
|----------|----------|
| **X-ray** | May be normal early; later: sclerosis, fragmentation, flattening |
| **MRI** | Most sensitive early; shows bone marrow changes |
| **Bone scan** | Decreased uptake in femoral head |

### Stages (Waldenstrom Classification)
1. **Initial/Necrosis:** Femoral head becomes dense
2. **Fragmentation:** Resorption and collapse
3. **Reossification:** New bone formation
4. **Remodeling:** Final shape determination

### Management
- **Orthopedic referral** (not emergent unless severe pain)
- Goals: Maintain ROM, contain femoral head in acetabulum
- Treatment based on age, extent of involvement
- Younger children (<6) have better prognosis

## Growing Pains vs Pathology

### Growing Pains: Diagnostic Criteria

**ALL of the following must be present:**
1. Age 3-12 years
2. Pain in bilateral lower limbs (not limited to joints)
3. Pain occurs in evening/night (never present upon waking)
4. Child does NOT limp
5. Normal physical examination
6. No systemic symptoms

### Characteristics of Growing Pains
- Location: Calves, anterior thighs, shins, popliteal fossa
- Timing: Late evening or awakens from sleep
- Duration: Minutes to hours, gone by morning
- Response: Massage, heat, reassurance help
- No functional limitation during day

### Red Flags: When It's NOT Growing Pains

| Red Flag | Suggests |
|----------|----------|
| Limp | Any organic pathology |
| Morning stiffness | JIA |
| Joint swelling | Arthritis |
| Unilateral pain | Trauma, infection, tumor |
| Pain during activity | Structural problem |
| Systemic symptoms | Infection, malignancy |
| Night pain waking child | Malignancy, infection |
| Age >12 or <3 years | Reconsider diagnosis |
| Point tenderness | Fracture, infection |
| Refusal to bear weight | Septic joint, fracture |

### When to Investigate
- If history is classic AND exam is normal: NO workup needed
- If ANY red flag: Full evaluation required

### Infants (<1 year)
| Condition | Key Features |
|-----------|--------------|
| Septic arthritis | Pseudoparalysis, fever, irritability |
| Osteomyelitis | Point tenderness, may be afebrile |

| Condition | Key Features |

| Toddler's fracture | Tibial spiral fracture, may not recall trauma |

| Condition | Key Features |
|-----------|--------------|

## Imaging Approach

### First-Line Imaging

#### Plain Radiographs
- **Indications:** Trauma, suspected fracture, SCFE, Perthes, tumor
- **Views:** AP and frog-leg lateral for hip; AP and lateral for other joints
- **Limitations:** May be normal early in infection, Perthes, JIA

#### Ultrasound
- **Indications:** Hip effusion, suspected septic joint
- **Advantages:** No radiation, real-time, guides aspiration, detects small effusions
- **Findings:** Effusion >5mm capsule-to-bone distance OR >2mm compared to other side
- **Limitation:** Cannot differentiate transient synovitis from septic arthritis

### Second-Line Imaging

#### MRI
- **Best for:** Osteomyelitis, early Perthes, JIA, tumor, soft tissue
- **Advantages:** Most sensitive and specific for bone/soft tissue pathology
- **Disadvantages:** Cost, sedation often needed in young children

#### Bone Scan
- **Indications:** Multifocal osteomyelitis, metastatic disease, Perthes
- **Limitation:** Less specific than MRI

### Imaging by Suspected Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | First-Line | Second-Line |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Septic arthritis | Ultrasound | MRI |
| Transient synovitis | Ultrasound | None (if classic) |
| Osteomyelitis | X-ray | MRI (preferred) |
| SCFE | X-ray (AP + lateral) | MRI if X-ray negative |
| Perthes | X-ray | MRI for early/staging |
| JIA | None initially | Ultrasound or MRI |
| Fracture | X-ray | CT or MRI |
| Malignancy | X-ray | MRI + bone scan |

## Laboratory Workup

### Basic Screening Panel (Most Cases)
| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| CBC with differential | Infection, malignancy (cytopenia) |
| ESR | Inflammation (slower response) |
| CRP | Inflammation (faster response) |
| Blood culture | If septic arthritis suspected |

### Extended Panel (Based on Suspicion)

#### If Septic Arthritis Suspected
- **Synovial fluid analysis:** Cell count, Gram stain, culture
- **Synovial PCR:** For Kingella in young children

| Synovial WBC | Interpretation |
|--------------|----------------|
| <2,000/uL | Non-inflammatory |
| 2,000-50,000/uL | Inflammatory (JIA, reactive) |
| >50,000/uL | Highly suspicious for septic |
| >100,000/uL | Very specific for septic |

#### If JIA Suspected
| Test | Notes |
|------|-------|
| ANA | Positive in 70% oligoarticular; uveitis risk |
| RF | Positive in RF+ polyarticular subtype |
| HLA-B27 | Consider if enthesitis-related suspected |
| Ophthalmology referral | For uveitis screening |

#### If Rheumatic Fever Suspected
- ASO titer (peaks 3-5 weeks post-infection)
- Anti-DNase B (peaks 6-8 weeks)
- Throat culture
- ECG (PR interval)
- Echocardiogram

#### If Lyme Suspected
- Lyme ELISA + Western blot if positive
- Rapid Sofia IgG if available

#### If Malignancy Suspected
- CBC with smear review
- LDH, uric acid
- Peripheral blood smear
- Consider bone marrow

### Labs That Do NOT Help in Primary Care
- ANA and RF: Not useful for screening; only if JIA already suspected
- HLA-B27: Only if ERA/spondyloarthropathy suspected
- Lyme serology: Only in endemic areas with appropriate presentation

### 5. Jones Criteria Calculator (Acute Rheumatic Fever)

**Purpose:** Assess probability of ARF in child with post-streptococcal symptoms

**Inputs:**

**Population Risk:**
- Low-risk (ARF incidence ≤2/100,000 school-age children)
- Moderate/High-risk (all others)

**Evidence of Prior Strep Infection:**
- Positive throat culture
- Elevated/rising ASO
- Elevated/rising anti-DNase B
- Recent scarlet fever

**Major Criteria:**
| Criterion | Low-Risk | High-Risk |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Carditis | Clinical or subclinical | Clinical or subclinical |
| Arthritis | Polyarthritis only | Mono/polyarthritis or polyarthralgia |
| Chorea | Yes | Yes |
| Erythema marginatum | Yes | Yes |
| Subcutaneous nodules | Yes | Yes |

**Minor Criteria:**
| Criterion | Low-Risk | High-Risk |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Fever | ≥38.5C | ≥38.0C |
| ESR | ≥60 mm/hr | ≥30 mm/hr |
| CRP | ≥3.0 mg/dL | ≥3.0 mg/dL |
| Prolonged PR | Yes (if no carditis) | Yes (if no carditis) |

**Diagnosis:**
- First episode: Evidence of strep + (2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor)
- Recurrence with prior RHD: 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor OR 3 minor

**Rules:**
- Cannot count polyarthritis AND polyarthralgia (only one joint criterion)
- Cannot count carditis AND prolonged PR (only one cardiac criterion)

## Decision Tree Structure Recommendations

### Primary Branch: Acute vs Chronic
```
Is this acute (<2 weeks) or chronic (>6 weeks)?
├── ACUTE → Focus on infection, trauma, reactive
│   └── Is child sick/febrile?
│       ├── YES → Septic arthritis pathway
│       └── NO → Transient synovitis vs mechanical
└── CHRONIC → Focus on inflammatory, orthopedic
    └── Morning stiffness present?
        ├── YES → JIA pathway
        └── NO → Growing pains vs orthopedic
```

### Secondary Branch: Age-Based
```
What is the age?
├── Infant → Septic/osteo, NAI
├── Toddler → Transient synovitis, Kingella, toddler's fx
├── School-age → Perthes, JIA, growing pains, Lyme
└── Adolescent → SCFE (must r/o), JIA-ERA, overuse
```

### Hip-Specific Decision Point
```
Hip pain in adolescent?
└── ALWAYS rule out SCFE first (even if knee pain)
    └── Obese? Externally rotated? → X-ray immediately
```
